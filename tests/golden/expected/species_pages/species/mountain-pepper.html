Growing Mountain Pepper in Australia

Mountain pepper (Tasmannia lanceolata) is a cool-climate shrub from the highlands of Tasmania, Victoria and southern New South Wales, prized for both its peppery leaf and its intensely hot dark berries. It is the bush food that breaks the usual rules: it is frost-hardy rather than frost-tender, it wants cool, moist, dappled conditions rather than heat, and it needs a male and a female plant to set berries. The state sections cover where its cool-climate needs are met and where summer heat makes it a struggle.

Male and female plants

Mountain pepper is dioecious, meaning male and female flowers are on separate plants, so you need at least one of each near one another to get berries. Female flowers are small with few petals and develop into the fruit; male flowers are larger, clustered and carry many petals. The leaf is peppery and can be harvested from any plant regardless of sex, so a single plant is still worth growing for leaf alone, but for the famous black pepperberries you must plant a pair. [Wikipedia] [Bronzewing Farm]

Cool, moist, dappled

This is a highland understorey shrub, so it wants the opposite of a hot dry border: cool conditions free of water stress, a moist humus-rich and slightly acidic soil, and dappled shade or part shade, with protection from hot afternoon sun in warmer districts. It is hardy to frost, wind and poor soils once settled, but it resents drought and baking heat, which set it back or kill it. Mulch heavily, keep the root zone cool and damp, and it grows into a dense shrub of two to five metres. [Bunnings] [Mallee Native Plants]

The leaf and the berry

Both the leaf and the berry are aromatic and pungent, building a slow, lingering heat. The leaf is harvested year round and dried or used fresh as a native pepper. Where a male and female grow together, small cream flowers in spring give way to red berries that ripen to a glossy purple-black over autumn; these are dried and ground as the hottest of the native pepper spices and should be used sparingly. Pick leaf as you prune, and harvest berries once they colour up fully. [Bronzewing Farm] [Tucker Bush]

Care and harvest

Keep young plants evenly moist and shaded while they establish, and feed lightly in spring with a native-safe fertiliser. Tip prune to keep the bush dense and to encourage the soft leaf you harvest. It is naturally slow to moderate growing and long-lived once it has the cool, damp position it wants. The single biggest mistake is planting it in a hot, dry, sunny spot, where it sulks; given shade and steady moisture it is hardy and trouble-free. [Bunnings] [AgriFutures]

Buying tips

Because you need both sexes for berries, buy plants sold and labelled as male and female, or buy several seedlings and keep a mix (seedlings are not sexed until they flower). If you only want the peppery leaf, a single unsexed plant is fine. Look for a healthy plant with the characteristic crimson young stems, and in a warm district choose the coolest, most sheltered, shadiest position you can offer it. [Tucker Bush] [AgriFutures]

Frequently asked questions

Do I need two mountain pepper plants to get berries?

Yes, for berries you do. The species carries male and female flowers on separate plants, so you need at least one of each growing near one another for the female to set fruit. If you only want the peppery leaf, which any plant produces, a single plant is enough.

Why is my mountain pepper struggling in a hot sunny spot?

It is a cool-climate highland plant that dislikes heat, drought and full exposure. Move it, or shade and mulch it heavily and keep the soil constantly moist. It wants dappled shade, cool roots and steady water, the opposite of a hot dry border, and will thrive once it has them.

Is mountain pepper a commercial crop?

Yes. It is one of Australia's established native-food crops, grown mainly in Tasmania and the southern uplands for both its peppery leaf and its hot dried pepperberry. That commercial interest is why home gardeners can buy named, sex-labelled plants rather than relying on unsexed seedlings.

Is mountain pepper frost-hardy?

Yes, unusually for a bush food it is genuinely frost-hardy and also takes wind and poor soils, which suits it to cold-climate gardens. Its weakness is the other end of the range: hot, dry, exposed conditions, which it does not tolerate.
