Growing Black Sapote in Australia

Across Australia the basics of growing black sapote are the same: full sun, free-draining soil, a warm and frost-free spot, a named grafted tree, and the discipline to pick the fruit hard and ripen it indoors. The chocolate pudding fruit is a tropical persimmon, so it rewards a leaner, drier hand than most fruit trees. The state sections cover the climate, regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes below apply anywhere black sapote will grow.

Choosing a variety

Black sapote is sold both as cheap unnamed seedlings and as grafted, named cultivars, and the grafted trees are the ones worth seeking out because they fruit far sooner and to a known size and seediness. The varieties grafted and sold in Australia include Maher (very large, flattened, few-seeded fruit on an unusually small and prolific tree to about 4 metres, which makes it the pick for a backyard), Bernecker (near-spherical fruit with only one or two seeds and superb eating quality), Mossman (the largest fruit of all, high in pulp and grown more for processing than dessert), Superb (a heavy-bearing North Queensland selection of small fruit that is often completely seedless), Ricks Late (which carries its crop later in the season) and Colossal (a very large, self-pollinating selection). You will also see Tahiti and Cocktail offered. Pick a named one and let the in-stock table above show what is available right now. Seedless or seeded? It depends on pollination

Whether your tree bears seedless fruit comes down to its flowers and what is growing nearby. Many seedlings and self-pollinating selections such as Colossal carry heavy crops, but the fruit holds one to five seeds. Some prized selections have female-only flowers and set seedless fruit when no compatible pollen reaches them, yet the very same tree will set a heavier crop of seeded fruit if a seedling or perfect-flowered tree blooms close by. So a pollinator lifts your yield but costs you the seedlessness. If seedless fruit is the goal, grow a known female-flowered selection (Superb is the usual choice) on its own, away from other black sapotes; if sheer quantity matters more, plant a pollinator alongside and accept the seeds. Planting and soil

Black sapote wants full sun and free-draining soil. It grows on a wide range of soils, does best on a moist sandy loam, and will even shrug off short spells of flooding, but it resents permanent waterlogging, so avoid low spots where water sits and plant on a low mound if your ground is heavy. A slightly acid to neutral soil of about pH 6 to 7 suits it. The tree is slow for its first three or four years, putting on perhaps 30 centimetres a year, then speeds up, so give a full-size tree 8 to 10 metres of room (less for compact Maher). Young trees are frost-tender and want a warm, sheltered position; mulch well but keep the mulch off the trunk. Container-grown trees go in best in the warm months (see our guide on when to plant). Water, feeding, and the shy-bearer trap

The commonest mistake with black sapote is kindness. A tree that is heavily fed and watered runs to lush leaf and bears shyly, while the heaviest croppers are often the half-neglected ones on leaner, drier ground. Water a young tree regularly to establish it, and irrigate any bearing tree through a real dry spell, but otherwise hold back. A simple, long-used routine is to lime, fertilise and lightly mulch once a year after harvest and then largely leave the tree alone, keeping soil moisture up only in severe dry weather. Go easy on nitrogen in particular, since it is what drives the leafy, fruitless growth. A leaner regime also gives smaller but noticeably richer fruit. [RFCA chocolate pudding fruit] [City of Darwin orchard]

Harvest: the calyx test, and never ripen on the tree

Black sapote is picked hard and green and ripened indoors, never left to ripen on the tree. The signal that a fruit is mature and ready to pick is the calyx, the green cap on top: while the fruit is filling, the cap sits tight against it, and when the cap loosens and its edges lift away from the fruit all round, that fruit is ready. The glossy bright-green skin also dulls toward olive and then a muddy green. Cut the fruit from the tree, never pull it, and lay the fruit out to soften over about three to six days at room temperature. Do not wait for soft fruit on the branch: a ripe black sapote is jelly-soft and splatters messily if it falls, and a tree-ripened fruit rarely comes good. [RFCA black sapote] [City of Darwin orchard] [RFCA chocolate pudding fruit]

Eating it, and the unripe warning

A black sapote is ready to eat only when it is completely soft, giving all over like an overripe persimmon and holding the dent of a finger. Cut it across the middle, lift out the few flat seeds, and scoop the dark, glossy pulp, which eats like a mild, sweet chocolate mousse and is lovely fresh with a squeeze of lime, with cream, or with a little rum or coffee liqueur; it also freezes well and bakes into cakes and ice cream. The warning matters: an unripe black sapote is not merely disappointing but astringent, caustic, bitter and an irritant, the same mouth-puckering tannin that marks an unripe persimmon, so wait for full softness and never serve it firm. [City of Darwin orchard] [Morton, Fruits of Warm Climates] [Meyer, ACOTANC 2001] [RFCA black sapote]

Buying tips

Buy a named, grafted black sapote rather than an unnamed seedling. A grafted tree fruits in about two years against five to seven for a seedling, comes true to a known fruit, and lets you match the variety to your plot, a compact Maher for a small garden or a big-fruited Mossman for the kitchen. Check the label for the cultivar name and, if seedless fruit matters to you, for whether it should be grown away from a pollinator. Good black sapote is not always easy to find and the grafted, named trees sell out, which is exactly why the in-stock table above is worth watching. Frequently asked questions

What does black sapote taste like?

Ripe black sapote is mild, sweet and low in acid, with a smooth texture often compared to chocolate mousse or a soft caramel custard, which is how it earned the name chocolate pudding fruit. It does not actually taste of cocoa; the appeal is the rich, pudding-like flesh rather than a strong flavour, and many people lift it with a squeeze of citrus.

Is black sapote a persimmon?

Yes, in the sense that it belongs to the same genus, Diospyros, as the orange persimmon, which is why some growers call it the black or chocolate persimmon. It is an evergreen, tropical member of that family rather than the deciduous, cool-climate kind, and it shares the persimmon habit of being harshly astringent until it is fully ripe.

Can I grow black sapote in a pot?

You can grow a compact grafted variety such as Maher in a large container for some years, which suits a courtyard or a frost-prone climate where you need to move it under cover in winter. Use a free-draining mix, water through the warm months because pots dry out fast, feed lightly so it does not run to leaf, and pot it up every couple of years.

How big does a black sapote tree get, and can I keep it small?

In ideal tropics the tree can reach 10 metres or more, but in most Australian gardens it stays smaller, and the grafted Maher selection naturally tops out at around 4 metres, which is why it is the usual choice where space is tight. Any black sapote also takes pruning after harvest to hold it to a height you can net and pick, so size need not put you off.
