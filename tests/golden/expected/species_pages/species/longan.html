Growing Longan in Australia

Wherever you grow longan in Australia the essentials are the same: a frost-free spot, deep free-draining acid soil, a named air-layered or grafted tree, and a cool, dry winter to switch the tree from leaf to flower. Longan is the close cousin of the lychee and grows much the same way, but it stores far better and a mature tree takes a little more cold. The state sections cover the climate, growing regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes below apply anywhere you can grow the tree.

Choosing a variety

Australian longans are sold as named cultivars, and the choice matters because they crop very differently here. Kohala, a Hawaiian selection, is the popular all-rounder: early, vigorous, sweet and mild, and the least fussy about a cool winter, so it is the one most likely to set fruit in a marginal year. The best-flavoured types are the late Thai selections, and they are widely sold: Haew is a crisp, sweet, high-quality table longan, Chompoo carries a faint pink tinge in crisp well-flavoured flesh, and Biew Kiew is a heavy, comparatively regular cropper with large crisp cream-coloured flesh. Steer clear of unnamed seedlings, since most of the longan first grown in Australia was thin-fleshed and poor, and treat Daw, the Thai favourite, with caution because it sets badly in Australian conditions year after year. Longan shares its needs and many of its pests with its close relative the lychee. [RFCA Walkamin trial] [RFCA the longan] [AgriFutures emerging fruits]

Pollination: do you need two trees?

A single longan will set a crop on its own. The tree carries both male and female flowers together on a large terminal panicle and is pollinated by insects, with the honey bee the main carrier, so what really lifts your crop is strong bee activity and warm, dry, still weather across the few weeks of flowering, not a second variety planted nearby. The catch is the cultivar rather than the partner: a few types set poorly here whatever you do, most notably Daw, so the surer path to fruit is a dependable variety served by plenty of bees. [BeeAware pollination] [RFCA Walkamin trial]

Planting and soil

Plant in full sun in deep, free-draining, slightly acidic soil (about pH 5.5 to 6.5) in a frost-free position with good shelter from wind. Longan grows fast and its wood is brittle, so young trees blow over or shed limbs easily: stake them firmly, and in cyclone country keep them low and consider mounding soil around the butt as Asian growers do. The tree hates wet feet and salty ground, and a young longan can die after only five to ten days in waterlogged soil, while on high-pH soils it shows nutrient deficiencies that need correcting. Mulch heavily to keep the shallow feeder roots cool and moist. Warm soil gets a new tree away fastest, so spring into early summer is the usual planting time (see our guide on when to plant). [UF/IFAS longan guide] [RFCA the longan] [RFCA longan notes]

Water and feeding

Longan is shallow-rooted, so keep the soil evenly moist under a thick mulch through the warm growing season, and above all while the fruit is filling, when going short cuts both size and yield. The exception is late summer through winter: easing right back on water then helps stop soft leafy growth and tips the tree toward flowering, the same job the cool dry season does. Feed along the lines used for lychees, leaning on potassium and going easy on nitrogen, since too much nitrogen drives leaves instead of flowers, and the trees run short of trace elements on poorer ground and repay a leaf or soil test. Drainage matters as much as watering, because the roots rot in soil that stays wet. [UF/IFAS longan guide] [RFCA Walkamin trial] [RFCA longan notes]

Irregular and biennial bearing

The hardest thing about longan is that it does not crop evenly. Even the better varieties tend to bear heavily one year and lightly or not at all the next, and Australian trials saw as few as three good crops in five or six seasons. Part of the cause is the cool dry winter the tree needs to flower, because a mild or wet winter simply skips a crop; part is a tree exhausting itself after a bumper year; and part is heavy post-harvest pruning that strips too much wood and leaves too little time for the new growth that carries next year's flowers. Prune lightly and promptly after picking, thin a very heavy set, and choose a comparatively regular variety such as Biew Kiew if steady cropping matters to you. [RFCA Walkamin trial] [RFCA longan notes] [UF/IFAS longan guide]

Harvest, storing and drying

Longan ripens in hanging clusters through late summer into autumn, broadly January to April in most districts and later where the season is cool. The fruit does not sweeten after picking, so harvest only once the brown skin is full and a sample tastes sweet (the flesh runs to roughly 18 to 21 percent sugar). The great advantage over lychee is keeping quality: the fruit holds for several weeks in the fridge at about 5 to 10 degrees, the skin darkening while the flesh stays good, and it freezes, cans and dries cleanly, with dried longan (the dragon's eye) a staple of Chinese cooking. A grafted or air-layered named tree usually starts bearing about three to five years after planting. [RFCA Walkamin trial] [RFCA longan storage] [RFCA the longan]

Buying tips

Buy a named, air-layered (marcot) or grafted tree, never an unnamed seedling, because longan does not come true from seed and a seedling gives unpredictable fruit. If you buy a grafted tree, look for a clean union and dark, glossy leaves, since longan is prone to graft incompatibility that leaves a stunted, dull-leaved tree; using a matched same-variety rootstock avoids it, which is one reason marcots are so popular. Frequently asked questions

How long until a longan tree fruits?

A grafted or air-layered (marcot) named variety usually starts bearing about three to five years after planting, with useful crops a little later. A seedling can take six to eight years, may never crop well, and will not come true to its parent, which is why propagated named stock is worth paying for.

Can I grow a longan tree in a pot?

While it is young, yes. A longan will live in a large pot for a few years, which lets you nurse it through cold winters or stand it in your sunniest corner, but it is a big, long-lived tree that crops best planted out in a warm spot. Treat a pot as a way to start, or to keep a tree alive in a cool district, rather than a permanent home for a bearing tree.

How big does a longan tree get, and can I keep it smaller?

Left alone a longan can reach 9 to 12 metres, but it takes pruning well and most backyard growers top it after harvest to hold it at a pickable 3 to 4 metres. Keeping it low also makes it far easier to throw a net over the canopy as the fruit ripens.

How is a longan different from a lychee?

They are close cousins grown much the same way, but a longan is brown-skinned and musky-sweet where a lychee is red and perfumed, with a bigger seed and slightly less juicy flesh. A longan also stores and dries far better than a fragile lychee, a mature tree shrugs off a little more cold, and it often wants a touch more winter chill to flower well.
