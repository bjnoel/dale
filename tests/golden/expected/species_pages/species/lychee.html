Growing Lychee in Australia

Wherever you grow lychees in Australia the fundamentals are the same: a frost-free spot, deep and free-draining soil, a named air-layered or grafted variety, and a cool, dry winter to switch the tree from leaf to flower. The state sections cover the climate, growing regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes below apply anywhere you can grow the tree.

Choosing a variety

Australian lychees are sold by named Chinese and Thai cultivars, and the choice matters more than with most fruit trees because some crop far more reliably than others. Kwai May Pink (also sold as Kwai Mai Pink, and historically as Bosworth No. 3) is the mainstay: small-seeded, very sweet, and one of the few that crops well almost every year from New South Wales to the far north. Wai Chee is a dependable late variety, Tai So is the old early commercial standard but can bear irregularly, and Bengal carries big, handsome fruit (around a large seed) in heavy crops, usually every second year. Salathiel is prized for eating quality and the most consistent fruit set of the lot. Erdon Lee is a large, late, high-quality fruit. Lychee shares its needs and many of its pests with its close relative the longan. [ALGA varieties] [RFCA Walkamin cultivars] [FAO lychee in Australia] [Cull, WANATCA Yearbook 7]

Seed size and fruit quality

Lychee quality comes down largely to the seed. A full, fat seed leaves only a thin shell of flesh, while a small shrivelled seed (growers call it a chicken tongue seed) leaves the fruit almost solid aril. The best eating varieties are the small-seeded ones: Salathiel sets a chicken tongue seed in nearly every fruit, Kwai May Pink is reliably small-seeded, and a few types are even sold as seedless. Large-seeded varieties such as Bengal and Brewster still taste good but give you less to eat per fruit, so weigh seed size against how heavily and reliably the variety crops. [RFCA Walkamin cultivars]

Pollination: do you need two trees?

A single lychee will fruit on its own. Each tree carries both functional male and functional female flowers, which open in succession on the same large panicle, so the tree is effectively self-fruitful and you do not need a second variety. What you do need is insects: lychee depends on bees to carry pollen between the flowers, so a hive or good wild bee activity, plus warm, still, dry days during the few weeks of flowering, does far more for your crop than planting a pollinator partner. [Erickson, WANATCA Yearbook 23] [FAO lychee in Australia]

Planting and soil

Plant in full sun in deep, free-draining, slightly acidic soil, in a frost-free position sheltered from strong wind (lychee wood is brittle and young trees resent exposure). The tree hates waterlogging and dislikes salty or strongly alkaline soils, so avoid low spots where water sits and improve heavy ground before planting. Mulch well to keep the shallow feeder roots cool and moist. Warm soil gets a new tree away fastest, so spring into early summer is the usual planting time (see our guide on when to plant). [FAO lychee in Australia] [Australian Lychee Growers Association]

Water and feeding

Lychees are shallow-rooted and will not tolerate drying out, so keep the soil evenly moist under a good mulch. The danger window runs from flowering through to harvest, when water stress drops both yield and fruit size (one Australian trial saw a tree's gross return fall from about A$250 to A$35 under a severe drought), so this is the worst time to let a tree go thirsty, while at the other extreme the roots rot in waterlogged ground, so plant high and never let water sit. Feed little and often through the warm season and lean on potassium, which research links to better flowering: a worked Australian backyard program for a seven-year-old tree splits roughly 350 g of nitrogen, 170 g of phosphorus and 210 g of potassium across the year as urea plus muriate of potash, with a dressing after harvest. Keep nitrogen modest, since too much pushes leafy growth instead of flowers, and do not pile on potassium year after year. Lychee likes a slightly acidic soil (about pH 5.5 to 6.5), runs short of trace elements easily, and repays a little boron on the red volcanic soils of the eastern districts; ease right off feeding in autumn so the cool winter can switch the tree from leaf to flower. [FAO lychee in Australia] [RFCA fertilising lychees] [FAO lychee flowering]

Harvest

Lychees ripen in clusters through the warmer months, broadly November to March depending on your region and variety. The catch is that lychee is not a fruit that ripens after picking: it will not get any sweeter off the tree, so pick only once the skin has fully coloured (most varieties turn pink to red) and a sample tastes sweet. The fruit is highly perishable, and the bright skin browns within a day or two of harvest even though the flesh underneath stays good, so refrigerate it, keep the humidity up, and eat it within a few days. [ALGA handling] [FAO lychee in Australia]

Buying tips

Buy a named, air-layered (marcot) or grafted tree, never a seedling. Lychees do not come true from seed, seedlings can take ten years or more to bear and may never fruit well, while a marcot or grafted named variety usually starts cropping in about three to five years. Buy clean stock too: the lychee erinose mite spreads on infested nursery plants, so check the leaves for puckered, felty galls and start with mite-free trees. Frequently asked questions

Can I grow a lychee tree in a pot?

While it is young, yes. A lychee will live in a large pot for a few years, which lets you shelter it from frost and cold winters, but it is a big, long-lived tree that crops best in the ground in a warm climate. Treat a pot as a way to get started, or to keep a tree going in a cool district, rather than a permanent home for a cropping tree.

How long until a lychee tree fruits?

An air-layered (marcot) or grafted named variety usually starts bearing in about three to five years, with worthwhile crops from around year six. Seedlings can take ten years or more and the fruit is unpredictable, which is why named, propagated stock is worth paying for.

Why won't my lychee flower or fruit?

Lychees need a cool, dry winter to flower: several weeks of cool nights (below about 20 degrees) while the buds are just on the move. A mild or humid winter, a warm wet spring, or a young tree in soft leafy growth will all give you a tree full of leaves and no fruit.

Do lychees ripen after they are picked?

No. Lychees are not climacteric, so they do not sweeten or improve once picked. Pick them only when the skin has fully coloured and the fruit tastes sweet. The skin browns within a day or two, but the flesh keeps for several days if you store the fruit cool and humid.
