Growing Fig in Australia

Figs are one of the most rewarding and forgiving fruit trees for Australian gardens: a Mediterranean crop that wants full sun, free-draining soil and a hot dry summer to ripen. The state sections cover the climate, regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes below apply anywhere in the country.

Choosing a variety

Almost every fig sold in Australia is a common (parthenocarpic) variety, so the choice comes down to flavour, colour and what you want to make rather than pollination. Dark-skinned figs include Black Genoa (large, rich and a reliable heavy cropper, a good all-rounder), Brown Turkey (vigorous, hardy and the usual first fig for a new gardener) and Preston Prolific (a Victorian variety with thick, sweet, creamy flesh). Green and amber-skinned figs include White Adriatic (the classic drying fig), White Genoa (tender, well-suited to cooler districts) and Excel (very sweet and resistant to splitting). For a pot or a small courtyard, dwarf types such as Sweet Temptation stay compact. Decide whether you want figs for fresh eating, drying or jam, then match a named variety from the in-stock table above. [SGA figs] [Daleys figs] [Bulleen Art Garden]

Pollination: do you need two trees?

No. The common figs that Australian nurseries sell are self-sufficient: they set a full crop on a single tree with no pollination and no fig wasp, so one tree is all you need. The famous fig and wasp partnership only matters for Smyrna and San Pedro figs, which must be pollinated through caprifigs (a process called caprification) and are not sold in the general trade; they were once grown in older South Australian districts where the wasp was established. A handful of specialty types, such as the variety Spanish Dessert, still need a pollinator, but every mainstream variety fruits on its own. [RHS figs] [The Food Forest] [Bulleen Art Garden] [WANATCA Yearbook 15]

Planting and soil

Figs want full sun and free-draining soil. They are famously unfussy, tolerating poor, sandy and mildly alkaline soils that defeat other fruit trees, but they will not accept wet feet, so avoid spots where water pools and plant on a low mound in heavy ground. Give them room: fig roots are vigorous and travel a long way near the surface, so keep a tree well clear of drains, paving and house foundations. In a small garden you can turn that vigour to your advantage by growing the tree in a large pot or a root-restricted bed, which curbs leafy growth and brings on fruit. Container-grown plants can go in for most of the year as long as you water them through summer (see our guide on when to plant). [The Food Forest] [RHS figs] [SGA figs]

Water and feeding

Figs are drought-hardy once established, but a tree carrying fruit needs steady, even moisture while the figs are swelling: let it dry out and the crop drops, then a sudden drink as the fruit ripens splits the skins. Water deeply and regularly through the dry months (a rough guide is about 25 mm a week from rain or irrigation), keep a thick mulch over the shallow roots, and ease off as the fruit colours up. Figs are light feeders that resent rich feeding, because too much nitrogen pushes soft leafy growth and slows ripening, so a fig in open, mulched ground often needs no feeding at all, and any feed should lean to potassium over nitrogen and go on from late winter to early spring. They like a near-neutral soil (about pH 6 to 6.5) improved with compost or well-rotted manure. A potted fig is the exception that does need feeding: keep the mix evenly moist and give it a high-potassium liquid feed every couple of weeks through the growing season, easing off once the figs start to ripen. [Sustainable Macleod] [UGA Extension figs] [RHS figs] [The Food Forest]

Pruning and the two crops

Figs can carry two crops. A light early (breba) crop forms on the tips of last year's wood and ripens in early summer, then the main crop sets on the current season's new growth and ripens from late summer into autumn. Prune in winter while the tree is bare: hard pruning removes the breba wood but drives strong new growth for a heavy main crop, and figs tolerate being cut back hard. Renewal pruning, where you cycle out the oldest trunk each winter and let a fresh shoot replace it, keeps a vigorous fig to about head height while it keeps fruiting on young wood. [Sustainable Macleod] [Rare Fruit Council archives] [SGA figs]

Harvest and ripening

Figs do not ripen once they are picked, so they must be left to ripen fully on the tree. A ripe fig softens, its neck bends so the fruit hangs down, the skin may split a little and a bead of nectar can form at the eye. Pick gently every day or two through the season, because ripe figs bruise easily and do not keep more than a couple of days. The milky sap from the stalk of a just-picked or pruned fig can irritate skin and lips, so wear gloves if you are handling a lot of fruit. [Clemson Extension] [SGA figs] [RHS figs] [The Food Forest]

Buying tips

Buy a named, cutting-grown tree rather than a seedling. Figs strike easily from dormant hardwood cuttings and come true to type that way, whereas seed-grown figs are a gamble and can turn out to be inedible wild (caprifig) types. Deciduous figs are sold bare-root through the dormant season, roughly May to August, often more cheaply than potted stock, while potted plants are available year round. Check the label for the variety name and use the in-stock table above to see what is available now and what it costs. [Daleys figs] [The Food Forest] [SGA figs]

Frequently asked questions

How big does a fig tree get, and can I keep it small?

A common fig can grow to 5 to 10 metres if left alone, but it is easily kept to 3 metres or so as a small tree, an espalier against a wall, or a pot specimen. For the smallest spaces, start with a naturally compact variety such as Sweet Temptation.

How long until a fig tree fruits?

Figs are quick. A cutting-grown named variety often carries a few fruit in its first or second year and crops well within three years, faster than most fruit trees. Restricting the roots in a pot can bring fruiting on sooner still.

Why won't my figs ripen after I pick them?

Figs do not ripen once they are off the tree, so a fig picked early stays hard and bland. Leave each fruit until it softens, droops on its stalk and smells sweet, then eat or use it within a day or two because ripe figs do not keep.

Will a fig tree's roots damage pipes or paving?

They can. Fig roots are vigorous and spread widely near the surface, so plant a tree several metres from drains, paths and foundations, or grow it in a large pot or a root-restricted bed to keep it in check (which also encourages fruiting).
