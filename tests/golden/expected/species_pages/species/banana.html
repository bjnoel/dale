Growing Banana in Australia

Across Australia the basics of growing a banana are the same: a warm, frost-free, wind-sheltered spot, rich and free-draining soil, plenty of water and feed, and clean, disease-free planting stock. Bananas are giant herbs rather than trees, and they crop fastest where it stays warm. The state sections below cover the climate, growing regions, season and the pests that matter where you are; the notes here apply anywhere in the country.

Choosing a variety

Most backyard bananas in Australia fall into a few groups. Cavendish types (Williams, plus the shorter Dwarf and Super Dwarf Cavendish) are the familiar supermarket banana and crop heavily, though the tall forms catch the wind. Lady Finger is a favourite home banana, sweeter with a thinner skin, while Ducasse (also sold as Sugar banana or Pisang Awak) and the compact Dwarf Ducasse are rich and full flavoured. Goldfinger is a hybrid valued for its disease resistance. Specialty types include Blue Java (Ice Cream), Red Dacca, Pisang Ceylon and the starchy cooking plantains. Disease is worth weighing up when you choose: Cavendish resists Panama disease race 1 but is the main victim of tropical race 4, whereas Lady Finger, Ducasse and Sugar bananas are all attacked by race 1, so where Panama disease is in the soil a resistant type is the safer pick. How a banana grows, fruits and spreads

A banana is one of the world's largest herbs, not a tree. What looks like a trunk is a pseudostem of tightly wrapped leaf bases growing from an underground corm. Each pseudostem grows for most of a year, throws a single flower stalk that becomes one bunch, and then dies. Before it does, it sends up suckers (followers) from the corm, and you keep one or two of these to carry the next crop, so a single planting becomes a long-lived clump that fruits year after year. Edible bananas are propagated this way, from suckers, corm pieces or laboratory tissue culture, rather than from seed, so every plant is a clone of its parent. Planting, soil and site

Give a banana the warmest, most wind-sheltered, frost-free position you have, in full sun. The big soft leaves shred in strong wind and tall plants blow over, so plant against a sunny wall or behind a windbreak. Bananas are gross feeders with shallow roots, so they want deep, rich, free-draining soil with plenty of compost and a thick mulch, and they dislike both drought and waterlogging. They make a fast leafy screen and grow well alongside other tropical fruit such as mango. Plant or pot them on through the warm months when the soil is warm (see our guide on when to plant). [DAF banana handbook]

Water and feeding

Bananas are thirsty and hungry. The large leaf canopy uses a lot of water, so they are best grown with regular irrigation and will not fill a good bunch if they dry out. They are also among the heaviest feeders you can grow: the main requirements are potassium and nitrogen, given little and often right through the warm growing season, backed up with generous compost or well-rotted manure and mulch. Commercial crops run on roughly 250 to 350 kg of nitrogen per hectare a year, with nitrogen and potassium applied regularly rather than in one hit; in the backyard the same principle holds, so feed steadily and keep the potassium up while the bunch is filling, guided by your soil rather than guesswork. [DAF banana handbook] [ABGC nutrient plan]

Harvest and ripening

A banana bunch is cut while it is still green. As the fruit fills, the sharp ridges along each finger round off and the fingers plump up, and that loss of angularity is the signal the bunch is mature enough to cut even though it is green. Bananas ripen better off the plant, so cut the whole bunch, hang it somewhere warm, shady and airy, and let it colour up (a ripe banana nearby speeds things along). Slipping a loose bag or proper bunch cover over the developing bunch improves the fruit and keeps birds and flying foxes off. Once the bunch is off, cut that pseudostem out at the base, since it will not fruit again, and let a follower take over. [DAF banana harvest]

Pests and diseases to know

Two diseases matter most. Banana bunchy top virus is spread by the banana aphid, cannot be cured and kills the plant, so infected plants must be destroyed, and in the affected parts of the eastern states there is a national program and a legal duty to report it. Panama disease is a soil fungus with no cure that survives for decades: race 1 attacks Lady Finger, Ducasse and Sugar bananas, while the newer tropical race 4 also brings down Cavendish. Leaf-spot diseases (yellow and black Sigatoka) are the main leaf trouble, and stripping off old or spotted leaves helps; insect pests include the banana weevil borer, rust thrips and spider mites. The best defence a home grower has is to start with clean, certified, disease-free plants and never bring in suckers from an unknown garden. Buying healthy plants

Always buy named, certified disease-free plants, ideally tissue-cultured stock, from a reputable nursery. Tissue-cultured plants are true to type and begin life free of the pests and diseases that ride along on backyard suckers, which is exactly why swapping bits and suckers between gardens is the classic way bunchy top and Panama disease spread. Moving banana plants is regulated in the banana states, so the plant you buy should carry the right clean-stock credentials. Check the label for the variety name and choose a type that suits your space and your disease risk; the in-stock table above shows the named varieties available right now and what they cost. Frequently asked questions

Do you need two banana plants to get fruit?

No. Edible bananas set their fruit without pollination (they are self-producing and seedless), so a single plant crops perfectly well on its own and never needs a second one as a pollinator. All it asks is to be left to replace itself for the following season.

How long until a banana plant produces its first bunch?

Usually about 12 to 18 months from planting for the first bunch, then roughly a bunch a year as each follower takes its turn. It is driven by warmth more than by the calendar, so a plant in the tropical north fruits faster than the same one in a cool-winter southern garden.

Can I grow a banana in a pot?

Yes, if you choose a dwarf variety and a large pot. A potted banana is hungry and thirsty, so give it a rich mix, frequent feeding and daily watering in summer, and it will reward you with a lighter bunch than the same plant in open ground. A pot also lets you shift a tender plant under cover for winter.

How big does a banana plant get, and can I keep it smaller?

A tall Williams or Lady Finger can reach four or five metres to the top of the bunch, while Dwarf and Super Dwarf types stay around two metres, which is far easier to net, pick and shelter from wind. You cannot prune a banana down like a tree, so the way to keep it low is simply to plant a dwarf variety.
