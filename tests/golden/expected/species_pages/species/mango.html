Growing Mango in Australia

Across Australia the basics of growing mango are the same: full sun, free-draining soil, a frost-free spot, and a cool, dry spell before flowering so the tree sets fruit instead of leaf. The state sections cover the climate, regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes below apply anywhere mango will grow.

Choosing a variety

Kensington Pride (often shortened to KP) is the classic Australian mango and still most of the crop. R2E2, a Queensland selection from the variety Kent, gives large, low-fibre fruit; Honey Gold is a popular modern Australian variety; and late types such as Keitt and Brooks stretch the season into autumn. Thai types like Nam Doc Mai, Khiew Sawoey and Chok Anan, and Indian types like Alphonso, Kesar and Mallika, suit warm gardens and are widely stocked. Decide on flavour and season first, then pick a named variety to match; the in-stock table above shows what is available right now. [Business Qld mango industry] [RFCA R2E2] [RFCA Kensington]

Seedling or grafted? The polyembryonic trick

Mango is unusual: many varieties are polyembryonic, meaning a seed produces several clones of the mother tree, so the seedlings come true to type. Kensington Pride and most South-East Asian mangoes (such as Nam Doc Mai) are polyembryonic, which is why a Kensington Pride seedling can fruit much like its parent. Indian and Florida varieties (Alphonso, Haden, Irwin, Keitt, Kent) are monoembryonic: their seedlings are unpredictable hybrids and must be grafted. For a known fruit, buy a named grafted tree, or a true-to-type seedling of a polyembryonic variety. [RFCA grafting mangos] [Hawaii CTAHR mango] [UF/IFAS mango]

Do you need two trees?

No. Most mango varieties are self-fertile, so a single tree will set fruit on its own. The small flowers are pollinated mainly by flies and other insects rather than honeybees, so a good crop depends far more on warm, dry, still weather during flowering than on having a second tree nearby. Planting and soil

Mango wants full sun and deep, free-draining soil; a sandy loam is ideal. It will not tolerate waterlogging, so avoid spots where water pools and plant on a mound if your soil is heavy. Young trees are frost-tender, so give them a warm, sheltered position and protect them over their first winters where light frosts occur. Container-grown trees can go in through the warmer months as long as you water them in well (see our guide on when to plant). [NT Government mango] [UF/IFAS mango] [DAF Qld mango kit]

Water and feeding

Water young trees regularly to get them established (a guide is up to 100 litres a tree a week over the first couple of years) and mulch with coarse straw kept off the trunk; mature trees are more drought-hardy but still crop better with steady water. A cool spell is what actually triggers a mango to flower, but running the tree a little dry from late autumn until flowering is thought to sharpen and synchronise that flowering, so growers ease off water and heavy nitrogen through winter, then water steadily again from flowering through fruit development and stop a few weeks before harvest. Feed with a complete fertiliser (the Queensland mango kit uses an N:P:K of about 15:4:11, at only 30 to 60 grams per young tree every six to eight weeks in the first year, building up with age), and put it on soil that is moist after watering rather than bone dry. Time the bulk of the nitrogen for just after harvest when the tree is flushing, and go easy on nitrogen at flowering and fruit set, since too much pushes leaves at the expense of fruit. Potassium is the key fruiting nutrient and is best split into several small doses through fruit fill, while boron and zinc are the trace elements that most often run short in mango (low boron shows as small holes in the leaves), so a soil and leaf test is the best guide to what your tree needs. [NT Agnote irrigation] [DAF Qld mango kit] [AMIA crop nutrition] [AMIA Qld flowering]

Harvest and ripening

Mangoes are picked mature but still firm and green, then ripened off the tree at room temperature (about 18 to 22 degrees). A grafted tree usually starts bearing in about three to five years, sometimes sooner in the tropics. Take care with the sap: when the stalk is snapped the first spurt is caustic, and it both burns dark marks into the fruit skin (sapburn, worst in Kensington Pride) and can irritate your skin, so pick with a clean snap and stand the fruit stalk-down to drain. [NT Government mango] [Business Qld harvest] [DAF Qld mango kit] [Hawaii CTAHR mango]

Pruning

Prune after harvest to keep the tree small and open. Mango flowers from the tips of its branches, so a compact, well-lit canopy carries plenty of fruiting terminals and is far easier to net, spray and pick. A backyard tree can be held to two or three metres with yearly pruning. [DAF Qld mango kit]

Buying tips

Buy a named grafted tree, or a true-to-type polyembryonic seedling such as Kensington Pride, rather than an unknown seedling that may never fruit to type. Dwarf and semi-dwarf selections (stocked as Irwin, Palmer, Bambaroo and King Thai dwarfs, and Little Gem) suit large pots and small gardens. Note that Calypso (the brand name for variety B74) and Honey Gold are trademarked, managed varieties grown under licence: Honey Gold trees do reach nurseries, but Calypso is sold as fruit and is rarely offered as a backyard tree, so the table above shows the named varieties you can actually buy now. Frequently asked questions

How big does a mango tree get, and can I keep it small?

A mango can grow into a large tree of 10 metres or more, but a yearly prune after harvest easily holds it to two or three metres, and dwarf varieties such as Little Gem stay smaller again, which suits a big pot or a small backyard.

How do I ripen mangoes at home?

Mangoes are picked firm and green and ripen on the bench over several days; a ripe one gives a little at the stem end and smells sweet. To hurry it along, sit the fruit in a paper bag with a banana, and do not refrigerate an unripe mango because cold stops it ripening properly.

How long until a mango tree fruits?

A grafted mango usually starts bearing in about three to five years, sometimes sooner in the tropics. Seedlings of monoembryonic varieties take longer and the fruit is unpredictable, which is why named, grafted stock is worth paying for.

Why does mango sap burn the fruit and my skin?

The first sap that spurts out when you snap the stalk is caustic. It causes dark sapburn marks on the skin (worst in Kensington Pride) and can irritate your own skin, so harvest with a quick snap, keep the sap off the fruit, and stand picked fruit stalk-down to drain.
