Growing Avocado in Western Australia

Growing avocados in Western Australia. WA has grown into one of the country's biggest avocado producers, led by the cool, high-rainfall Southern Forests of the south-west, with the main districts around Pemberton and Manjimup.

Climate fit

The south-west has a Mediterranean climate of hot dry summers and cool wet winters. Avocados need a frost-free site, because young trees are tender and even mature trees only shrug off brief cold to about minus 4 degrees, so the coldest inland pockets are risky. The cool, wet spring of the deep south-west can also make flowering and fruit set less reliable than in the warm eastern states, which is why growers there favour sheltered, north-facing slopes. [Business Queensland growing] [California Avocado Growers cold]

Where avocados grow in WA

The great majority of WA's avocados come from the Southern Forests region of the south-west, centred on Pemberton and Manjimup and reaching across to Donnybrook and the Busselton area. Pemberton on its own is one of the largest single avocado districts in the country. Newer plantings have spread onto the warmer Swan Coastal Plain to the north. [ABS horticulture] [Avocados Australia facts at a glance]

Harvest window in WA

WA fruit reaches the market later than the eastern states, mostly through the warmer half of the year, which helps keep Australian avocados on the shelf the year round. The long, cool growing season ripens the fruit slowly, and growers leave it hanging on the tree until they are ready to pick. [Avocados Australia facts at a glance] [FAO avocado]

Pests and diseases in WA

Phytophthora root rot is the main disease wherever avocados grow, WA included, so drainage and a tolerant rootstock matter. WA also has a headache of its own, the six-spotted mite, which has caused severe defoliation in orchards around Pemberton since 2014. On the insect side, WA has Mediterranean fruit fly across the state, although avocado is only a low-susceptibility host thanks to its thick skin. Importantly, WA is free of the Queensland fruit fly that troubles the eastern states, and works hard to keep it out. Across Australia the basics of growing an avocado are the same: full sun, perfectly drained soil, the right variety (or a pair), and shelter from frost and wind while the tree is young. Avocados are thirsty but hate wet feet, so drainage is the make-or-break decision. The state sections below cover the climate, regions, harvest timing and pests where you are; the notes here apply anywhere in the country.

Choosing a variety

Almost all the fruit sold in Australia is Hass, a pebbly-skinned variety that turns purple-black as it ripens, but home growers can choose from dozens of types. Avocados come from three races: Mexican types are the most cold-hardy and have anise-scented leaves, West Indian types are tropical and the most salt-tolerant, and Guatemalan types sit in between, with Hass and Fuerte both being Guatemalan and Mexican hybrids. For a cool or frost-prone garden, lean on the hardier Mexican-race varieties such as Bacon, Fuerte, Zutano and Mexicola. Reed is a large, round, late-season avocado, Shepard is a green-skinned type, and Wurtz (usually sold as a dwarf) stays small enough for a big pot. Pollination: do you need two trees?

Avocado flowers are unusual: each one opens as a female first, closes, then reopens later as a male, and varieties are grouped by the timing. Type A varieties (such as Hass and Reed) are female on the first morning and male the next afternoon, while Type B varieties (such as Fuerte and Shepard) are female in the afternoon and male the following morning. Planting one of each type puts pollen about when the other tree is receptive, which lifts fruit set, and bees do the carrying. A single tree can still crop on its own, so one is fine if that is all you have room for. The catch is temperature: in cool weather the female and male phases drift out of step and bees fly less, so cold districts set less reliably than warm ones. Planting and soil: drainage comes first

More backyard avocados are killed by wet soil than by anything else. The tree is very sensitive to poor drainage and to Phytophthora root rot, a soil-borne disease that thrives in waterlogged ground, so the most important thing you can do is plant into free-draining soil, on a low mound or raised bed if your soil is heavy. Give it full sun and shelter from strong wind, which snaps the brittle limbs and scars the fruit. Aim for a soil pH of about 5.5 to 6.5. Young trees are frost-tender, so in cool areas plant in spring once frosts have passed and protect the trunk for the first winter or two. The roots are shallow and easily disturbed, so plant gently and avoid digging around the tree later (see our guide on when to plant). Water and feeding

Avocado feeder roots sit in the top 15 centimetres of soil, which shapes both watering and feeding. Mulch the root zone thickly to keep it cool and moist, but hold the mulch back from the trunk so the collar does not rot. Water young trees through their first summers, and keep the water up to bearing trees through flowering and fruit fill, while never letting the soil stay soggy, since that invites root rot. Avocados are also salt-sensitive, so where the water or soil is salty a West Indian-type rootstock copes best. For feeding, nitrogen and potassium are the main nutrients and the rule is small and frequent: little doses suit the shallow roots far better than one big feed, and too much nitrogen just drives leafy growth at the expense of fruit, so go steady and taper off through late summer so the wood hardens. The commercial benchmark for a mature Hass is roughly 110 kilograms of nitrogen and 80 kilograms of potassium per hectare a year; for a backyard tree that means a complete fruit or citrus fertiliser given several times across the warm season (young trees more often, in smaller amounts). Avocados are prone to zinc and boron shortages, and to iron deficiency (yellowing leaves) on alkaline soils, so use a fertiliser that includes trace elements. The other job is root-rot prevention: phosphonate (phosphorous acid), applied as a spray or trunk injection, with gypsum under the canopy, is the standard defence. Harvest and ripening

Avocados do not soften on the tree. They reach maturity hanging there but only start to ripen once they are picked, so the tree acts as its own store, holding fruit for weeks or months until you want it. Maturity is judged by the oil or dry-matter content of the flesh, not by colour: the Australian standard is at least 23 percent dry matter for Hass and 21 percent for Shepard. A mature fruit ripens at room temperature in about a week to a fortnight without shrivelling; sit it in a bowl with a banana to speed it up, or in the fridge to slow it down. Because so many varieties are grown across such a spread of climates, Australian avocados are on sale all year round. Buying tips

Buy a named, grafted tree rather than a cheap seedling. Avocados do not come true from seed, so a seedling is a gamble that grows large and bears late, while a grafted named variety fruits sooner and to type. If you have room for two, pick a Type A and a Type B (for example Hass with Fuerte, Shepard or Edranol) to improve fruit set; if you have room for one, a single tree will still crop. For a courtyard or balcony, choose a dwarf such as Wurtz and grow it in a large pot. On heavy or wet-prone soil, ask for a tree on a vigorous, Phytophthora-tolerant rootstock such as Velvick. Frequently asked questions

How long does an avocado tree take to fruit?

A grafted tree usually bears its first decent crop about three to four years after planting, then builds from there. One grown from a pip can take far longer, sometimes a decade, and may never bear well, which is why a grafted named variety is worth the extra cost. Steady water and feeding while the tree is young brings that first crop on sooner.

Can I grow an avocado in a pot or a small garden?

Yes, if you pick a naturally small variety. Dwarf types such as Wurtz stay around three to four metres and live happily in a large, deep container of free-draining mix on a sunny patio. Containers dry out and lose nutrients quickly, so water and feed regularly in modest amounts, and because the roots dislike being disturbed, pot on gently and not too often. Expect a lighter crop than an in-ground tree.

Why is my avocado flowering but not setting fruit?

This is the most common avocado complaint, and usually it is the weather. A cold snap during flowering throws the male and female stages out of step and keeps the bees grounded, so the blossom goes unpollinated. A very young tree, or a lone one in a chilly garden, also sets less, and even a thriving tree naturally sheds the great majority of its blossom and pinhead fruit, keeping only a few. Warmth, a partner tree of the opposite type, and patience usually turn it around.

Can you grow an avocado from a seed?

You can, and a pip sprouted on a windowsill makes a handsome houseplant. What it will not do is reliably give you good fruit: seed-grown trees do not come true to the parent, grow very large, and can take many years to bear, if ever. For fruit you can count on, start with a grafted named variety.

Why is my avocado dropping its fruit?

Some drop is normal, because the tree sets far more than it can carry and thins itself, especially a few weeks after flowering. Heavier losses usually trace to stress: a tree that dried out or cooked in a hot, dry spell while the fruit was small, or watering that swings between bone-dry and soaked. Even, deep watering and a thick mulch through spring and summer hold more of the crop on the tree.

Which avocado varieties suit Perth and the south-west?

Hass is the mainstay and does well given a warm, sheltered spot. In a cold or frost-prone garden, add a hardier Bacon, Fuerte or Zutano, which take the cold better than Hass. For a courtyard, a dwarf Wurtz in a large pot is the easy choice.

Do avocados cope with Perth's sandy soils?

Yes, the free-draining sands of the coastal plain actually suit avocados, which hate wet feet, but sand dries and leaches fast. Mulch thickly, water young and bearing trees through the dry summer, and feed in small, regular amounts so the nutrients are not washed away before the roots use them.
