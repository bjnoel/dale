Fruit Tree Rootstock Guide (Australia)

The rootstock decides how big a fruit tree grows, how soon it fruits, and how it copes with your soil and pests. A plain-English guide to peach, plum and apple rootstock for Australian growers and grafters, with where to buy it and how to grow your own. Updated DATE.

What a rootstock is, and why it matters

Nearly every fruit tree you buy is two plants in one: a named fruiting variety (the scion) grafted onto a separate root system (the rootstock) [Daleys rootstock]. The rootstock is not just roots. It sets how big the tree grows, how soon it fruits, and how well it stands up to your soil, drainage, pests and diseases. Choosing the rootstock is half of choosing the tree, and for a grafter it is the half you get to decide.

On this page: Peach rootstock · Plum rootstock · Apple rootstock, plus where to buy rootstock in Australia and how to get it into the quarantine states.

Peach rootstock

Almost every peach and nectarine sold is a named variety budded onto a rootstock, and the rootstock decides how the tree copes with your soil, your pests and your water. Most Australian peach rootstocks are peach seedlings chosen for vigour and root-knot nematode resistance, with a peach-almond hybrid for limey ground and a few size-controlling hybrids where you want a smaller tree. Peaches are also the one stone fruit that comes fairly true from seed, so a peach stone is the classic home-raised rootstock.

Grow your own peach rootstock

Peaches come fairly true from seed, so a stone from a good peach makes a serviceable rootstock, which is why home grafters so often raise their own. The seedling will not be identical to its parent, but for a rootstock that does not matter; you bud your chosen variety onto it.

Raising a seedling from a stone

Clean the flesh off the stone, then give the seed a cold, moist stratification of roughly three months (about 98 to 105 days) at around 1 to 5 degrees, for example in damp sand or a sand and peat mix in the fridge. Sow the stratified seed about 5cm (2 inches) deep in spring once the cold period is done. [Iowa State Extension] [Philadelphia Orchard Project]

From seedling to grafted tree

A vigorous peach seedling is usually ready to bud or graft within a season or two, once the stem reaches roughly pencil thickness. Bud in late summer or graft your named variety in late winter. Commercial growers never fruit the seedling itself, because seedlings are too variable to sell as a known variety, but that same seedling is exactly what they bud a named variety onto. [FAO Australia] [Philadelphia Orchard Project]

Plum rootstock

Plums are grafted onto plum rootstocks chosen mostly for soil: Myrobalan for a strong all-round tree, Marianna where the ground is heavy and wet, and a peach rootstock in warm low-chill districts. Unlike peach, plum does not come true from seed, so the clonal plum rootstocks are grown from hardwood cuttings rather than stones, which also makes them the easiest fruit-tree rootstock to strike at home.

Grow your own plum rootstock

Plum does not come true from seed the way peach does, so the clonal plum rootstocks (Myrobalan and Marianna) are propagated from hardwood cuttings, which strike readily. That makes plum rootstock the easiest to raise at home for a grafter.

From hardwood cuttings

Take cuttings in winter while the wood is dormant, about 15 to 30cm (6 to 12 inches) long, dip the cut end in rooting hormone, and strike them in a free-draining mix. Marianna and Myrobalan both root well this way, which is how nurseries reproduce them. [Grow Organic] [UC Davis Foundation Plant Services]

Why not seed

A plum grown from seed rarely matches its parent, so seedlings are unpredictable; a cutting-grown clone of a known rootstock gives a uniform, predictable tree instead. Once the cutting has rooted, graft your chosen variety on in early spring as the sap starts to rise. [Grow Organic]

Apple rootstock

Apple is the rootstock story most gardeners actually meet, because the Malling (M) and Malling-Merton (MM) rootstocks set the size of the tree: the same variety can be a 1.5m courtyard tree or a 4m orchard tree depending only on the stock it sits on. Apples do not come true from seed and these dwarfing stocks are clones, not seedlings, so you cannot raise an MM106 from a pip; grafters buy named rootstock. Grow your own apple rootstock

Apple is the exception to grow-your-own. You cannot raise a dwarfing apple rootstock from seed, because apples do not come true from seed and the M and MM stocks are clones. If you want size control, you buy the named rootstock; growing your own only gives you a full-size seedling.

Why seed does not work

A pip from a good apple grows into a different tree, usually with poor fruit, and always full sized. It cannot carry the dwarfing you are after, so a seedling is only useful as a vigorous, standard-size rootstock, never as an MM106 or M9. [UNH grafting guide]

How clonal rootstock is made (stool beds)

Nurseries reproduce the M and MM clones by mound layering, also called stooling: a mother plant is cut low, the new shoots are mounded with soil or sawdust so they root at the base, then in autumn the rooted shoots are lifted as rootstock liners. It is the most common way apple rootstocks are propagated, and it is why the stock stays genetically identical. [UF stool layering] [How rootstocks are grown]

For the home grafter

Buy a named clonal rootstock (MM106 is the common all-round backyard choice in Australia), then graft your variety on in late winter or bud it in late summer. Australian suppliers sell M and MM stock singly or in small bundles, so a hobby grafter can get a handful. [Daleys rootstock guide] [Heritage Fruit Trees]

Where to buy rootstock in Australia

Rootstock is a niche, seasonal product, so the retail options are few. Daleys Fruit Tree Nursery in NSW sells apple, plum and peach rootstock as single potted plants [Daleys rootstock], and Heritage Fruit Trees sells apple and pear rootstock in bundles of three, as dormant bare-root plants for winter grafting or late-summer budding [Heritage Fruit Trees]. Beyond those, most rootstock moves through the trade: Olea Nurseries in Manjimup, WA grows the full deciduous range (apple, plum, peach, and more) but supplies commercial growers and garden centres only [Olea Nurseries WA], and Fleming's is wholesale and does not sell to the public [Fleming's Nurseries].

How rootstock is sold (and why we do not track it)

Rootstock is sold as dormant bare-root whips or liners in winter, often in fixed bundles or wholesale minimums, and usually ordered by phone, email or an order form rather than bought online. Unit sizes are inconsistent between sellers (single pots at Daleys, bundles of three at Heritage), and much of the supply is commercial-only. There is no continuously priced retail listing to compare day to day, which is exactly why treestock tracks finished, grafted trees and keeps rootstock as a maintained reference page instead of a live tracker [Heritage Fruit Trees].

Getting rootstock into WA, NT and Tasmania

Every state and territory sets its own plant-import quarantine conditions [Interstate Quarantine], and Western Australia, the Northern Territory and Tasmania are the hard ones. To enter WA, a plant must be listed as permitted on the Western Australian Organism List, and in most cases it has to be chemically treated and certified by the exporting state before it crosses [Quarantine WA]. Private importers can apply to bring in fewer than 20 plants that skip some treatments, but only washed free of all soil and potting mix and inspected on arrival [Quarantine WA]. In quarantine terms rootstock counts as carrier nursery stock, so it can only move interstate under treatment, inspection and a Plant Health Assurance Certificate [WA ICA-62]. This is why many eastern nurseries simply will not post to WA, SA or Tasmania: one Victorian bare-root nursery ships Australia-wide except South Australia, WA and Tasmania, citing quarantine difficulties [Yalca Fruit Trees]. For a WA grower, the way around interstate quarantine is to buy from a WA nursery, though the main WA rootstock supplier is wholesale only [Olea Nurseries WA].

Frequently Asked Questions

Where can I buy fruit tree rootstock in Australia?

Two nurseries sell rootstock direct to the public: Daleys Fruit Tree Nursery in NSW sells apple, plum and peach rootstock as single potted plants, and Heritage Fruit Trees sells apple and pear rootstock in bundles of three as dormant bare-root plants for winter grafting. Most other rootstock is wholesale, sold to commercial growers and garden centres only. Olea Nurseries in Manjimup WA carries the full deciduous range (apple, plum, peach) but to commercial growers only, and Fleming's does not sell to the public at all. Rootstock is a winter, bare-root product, usually ordered by phone, email or an order form rather than bought online day to day.

Can I get rootstock or a fruit tree into Western Australia?

It is restricted, not impossible. To enter WA a plant must be listed as permitted on the Western Australian Organism List, and in most cases it must be chemically treated and travel with plant health certification from the exporting state. Private importers can apply to bring in fewer than 20 plants that skip some treatments, but they must be washed free of all soil and potting mix and inspected on arrival. In quarantine terms rootstock is carrier nursery stock, so it needs a Plant Health Assurance Certificate to cross. This is why many eastern nurseries simply will not post to WA, and to the Northern Territory and Tasmania, which apply their own conditions. A WA grower who wants to avoid interstate quarantine buys from a WA nursery, though the main WA rootstock supplier is wholesale only.

Can I grow peach rootstock from a supermarket peach stone?

Yes. Peaches come fairly true from seed, so a stone from a good peach makes a usable rootstock. Give it about three months of cold, moist stratification in the fridge, sow it in spring, and bud or graft your chosen variety onto the seedling once it reaches pencil thickness. A grafted, named tree still fruits sooner and to type, so seed-raising is for the hobby grafter, not a shortcut to fruit.

Which peach rootstock suits sandy Perth soil?

A root-knot-nematode-resistant peach seedling such as Nemaguard is the classic choice for sandy soils, because root-knot nematode is the main sandy-soil problem for peaches. It prefers free-draining ground, so keep the water up in summer. On limey or alkaline sand, the peach-almond hybrid GF677 avoids the iron chlorosis that yellows a peach seedling.

Can I grow plum rootstock from a cutting?

Yes, and it is the easiest fruit-tree rootstock to raise at home. Myrobalan and Marianna strike readily from hardwood cuttings taken in winter, about 15 to 30cm long, with the cut end dipped in rooting hormone. Plum does not come true from seed, so cuttings, not stones, are the way to reproduce a known plum rootstock.

Which plum rootstock is best for heavy clay?

Marianna copes with heavy, wet ground better than Myrobalan, which is why nurseries reach for it on poorly drained soil. The trade-off is that Marianna is very susceptible to bacterial canker and is incompatible with peaches, nectarines and some almonds, so it is a plum-and-apricot rootstock, not a universal one.

Can I grow apple rootstock from seed?

Not the dwarfing kind. Apples do not come true from seed, and the M and MM rootstocks that control tree size are clones, propagated by mound layering (stooling), not grown from pips. A seedling apple can be used as a rootstock, but it gives a full-size, variable tree with no size control, so grafters buy named clonal rootstock instead.

What is the best apple rootstock for a backyard?

MM106 is the usual all-rounder in Australia: about 65% of full size, resistant to woolly aphid, and self-supporting after a few years. The catch is that it is prone to collar rot on wet, heavy soils, so if your ground stays wet, or you want a smaller tree and can give it a permanent stake, choose a dwarf such as M9 or M26 instead.
