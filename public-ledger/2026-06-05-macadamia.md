# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Macadamia growing guide: Australia's own native nut, with three myths corrected

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for macadamia, the only Australian native plant
grown as a major commercial food crop. Like the others, it replaces the generic blurb on
/species/macadamia.html and on the buy-macadamia-trees-[state] pages with scannable, cited,
state-aware advice, and it leans first on Benedict's own horticultural archives: the WANATCA
yearbooks (including "The future for macadamia nuts in Western Australia" by David Noel, the
association's founder) and the Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives.

The species page was already indexed but ranking on a thin, generic blurb, and that blurb had real
problems, so this is a good page to make richer and more trustworthy. It ties every variety it
recommends to what is actually for sale: the backyard hybrid Beaumont, the patented Australian "A"
series bred by Henry Bell (A4, A16, A38, A203), the older Hawaiian numbers (246/Keauhou, 344, 816,
849), and the dwarf forms for pots (A16, Lotsa Nuts).

### Three things the guide gets right that the old blurb did not
- Pests: the old blurb called the macadamia felted coccid "the main pest in Australia." It is not.
  The felted coccid is a native Australian scale that is usually minor here (kept down by its natural
  enemies); it is the destructive invader only overseas, in Hawaii and South Africa. The genuine
  flagship insect pest is the macadamia nut borer. The guide also corrects the common worry that
  macadamias get fruit fly: the hard, woody shell means the nut is not a fruit fly host at all.
- Phosphorus: the blurb said macadamias are "sensitive to phosphorus, use a low-phosphorus native
  fertiliser." The truth is more useful. A low-phosphorus native blend is a fine default for a
  backyard tree (that is what the Australian Macadamia Society recommends), but it is a myth that a
  macadamia must never see phosphorus: commercial orchards feed it phosphorus guided by soil tests,
  and it tolerates levels that would harm an ornamental banksia. The real hazard is a big excess of
  phosphorus, such as old vegetable ground loaded with superphosphate.
- Buying into WA: the blurb said "no significant quarantine restrictions apply." That is misleading
  for moving live plants. Western Australia regulates all interstate nursery stock (it must be
  permitted on the WA Organism List, and usually treated and certified), so many eastern nurseries
  will not post live macadamia trees to WA. The in-stock table is filtered to the sellers that can.

### Shipped (pending review)
- One state-invariant core (the two edible species, choosing a variety, pollination and why to plant
  more than one, planting and shelter from wind, water and feeding, harvesting and drying the nut,
  eating and safety including the toxicity to dogs, and the native-heritage conservation angle) plus
  four genuinely different state overlays. Western Australia is the flagship: the warm, dry,
  Mediterranean southwest grows macadamias well with irrigation, with trees fruiting at Kings Park
  and the University of Western Australia for the best part of a century. Queensland is now the
  country's biggest producer (Bundaberg overtook the New South Wales heartland in 2016). New South
  Wales is where the industry was born, in the Northern Rivers around Lismore in the 1880s. Victoria
  is honest about being too cold for a reliable crop, a backyard curiosity at best.
