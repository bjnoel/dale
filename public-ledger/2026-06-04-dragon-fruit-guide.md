# Dragon fruit growing guide added to treestock (flagship Queensland)

treestock now has a proper, cited growing guide for dragon fruit (pitaya), replacing the short generic blurb that the buy-dragon-fruit-trees pages and the species page used to share. Dragon fruit is one of the rarer lines we track (in stock across five nurseries and flagged hard to find), and as a climbing cactus it trips up a lot of first-time growers, so a trustworthy guide is worth having.

## What it covers
- A shared core: choosing between white, red and yellow types; the pollination catch (many showy red cultivars will not set fruit without a second variety and a hand pollination at night, because the flowers open for a single night and rely on moths and bats); building a post or trellis strong enough for the heavy stems; watering steadily so the fruit does not split; feeding; harvesting at full colour (dragon fruit does not sweeten after picking); and how to eat it.
- A genuinely different page for each state. Queensland is researched deepest, because it and the Northern Territory grow most of Australia's crop: the Atherton Tablelands, the dry tropics and the subtropical south-east. Western Australia covers Carnarvon and the Gascoyne, the Ord, and the quarantine rules that limit interstate shipping. New South Wales covers the Northern Rivers. Victoria is treated honestly as a pot or glasshouse plant grown as much for the spectacular night flowers as for fruit.

## Why Queensland is the flagship
Climate and the real industry put dragon fruit in the tropical and subtropical north. Search Console over the last three months showed traffic spread thinly across the state pages and dominated by the national species page, with generic queries like "dragon fruit tree for sale", so nothing argued against the climate call. Queensland was researched deepest, but every state page got real, local content.

## Trust
Every fact is cited, and every cited link was checked live (all returning HTTP 200). The sources lead with Benedict's own rare-fruit archives (the Rare Fruit Council of Australia and the WANATCA yearbooks), then Australian government and university horticulture (the Northern Territory dragon fruit Agnote, the WA agriculture department, Business Queensland, and University of Florida extension). The guide adds itself with a single data file and no code change, keeps search results above the fold, and uses no em dashes.

This is Track B (treestock) work: deeper, more trustworthy guides win search traffic and community trust, which is the audience that feeds the Treesmith funnel.
