# Public Ledger — 2026-06-02

## Olive guides now cite and cross-link Benedict's own horticultural archives

treestock's growing guides now draw on two archives Benedict hosts: WANATCA (the West Australian
Nut and Tree Crop Association, wanatca.org.au) and the Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives
(rfcarchives.org.au). Because these are owned domains, they work as first-party citations and
"Further reading" cross-links: better authority, no link rot, and readers stay within Benedict's
sites.

### Shipped
- The olive pages now cite Stan Kailis, "Growing Olives in Western Australia" (WANATCA Yearbook 22,
  1998), a WA-specific source by the recognised authority, and carry a Further reading block linking
  the Kailis article and the Rare Fruit Council's olive articles. Owned-site links are followed;
  third-party links are marked nofollow.
- A reusable index (build_archive_index.py, writing archive_links.json) maps the Rare Fruit
  Council's species-organised articles (about 30 species, including mango, custard apple, jaboticaba,
  fig and durian) to treestock species, so every future species guide auto-pulls relevant first-party
  cross-links. WANATCA yearbook articles are surfaced as a curation aid (their keyword matches are
  looser, so they are added by hand).

  URLs verified live (HTTP 200). No em or en dashes.

## Lychee is the second species with a full, cited, per-state growing guide

After olive, lychee now has the rich treatment: a guide that gives Western Australia, Queensland, New
South Wales and Victoria genuinely different advice instead of one shared blurb. Lychee was a good
second pick because it has the deepest Rare Fruit Council archive of any species and its states differ
sharply: Queensland grows about nine in ten of Australia's lychees, New South Wales picks last and only
on the warm far north coast, Western Australia can fruit it in the tropical north (around Kununurra)
but only erratically near Perth, and Victoria is honestly too frosty for it.

### Shipped
- A new lychee guide covering variety choice (Kwai May Pink, Wai Chee, Tai So, Salathiel, Bengal and
  Erdon Lee, all tied to what is actually in stock), the unusual lychee seed-size quality story, why
  the tree needs a cool dry winter to flower, planting, water, harvest and how to buy clean stock.
- The flowering, variety and propagation facts come first from Benedict's own RFCA and WANATCA
  archives, then cross-checked against FAO, the Australian Lychee Growers Association, Business
  Queensland and Western Australia's DPIRD. Further reading links two WANATCA yearbook articles and
  the Rare Fruit Council's lychee articles.
- Fixed a quiet bug while here: the species pages were not stripping en dashes out of nursery product
  titles, so names like "Lychee - Jean Hang" rendered with the wrong dash. That is fixed for every
  species page now, with a test to keep it fixed.

### Verification
- Full test suite 237 tests pass (14 new lychee checks plus a dash regression test). Every cited and
  further reading link verified live (HTTP 200). No em or en dashes. Each state page is unique, with
  no region names leaking between states.

## Fig growing guide: per-state-unique and archive-cited (second species after olive)

treestock now has a full, cited fig (Ficus carica) growing guide, the second species after olive to
get the per-state treatment. The buy-fig-trees pages for WA, QLD, NSW and VIC each carry a genuinely
unique overlay (climate fit, local growing regions, harvest window, and pests) over a shared, cited
core (variety choice, pollination, planting, water, pruning, harvest, and buying), so the four state
pages are no longer near-duplicates.

### Shipped
- tools/scrapers/growing_guides/fig.json: 23 sources, with every cited and Further-reading URL
  verified live (HTTP 200). First-party archives are preferenced: the WANATCA yearbook article on the
  fig and its wasp, and the Rare Fruit Council's renewal-pruning article, are cited and cross-linked,
  with DPIRD (fig is listed as a high-susceptibility Mediterranean fruit fly host) and Business
  Queensland as the government anchors.
- Flagship state is WA on climate reality: figs are a Mediterranean crop that crops best in Perth's
  dry summers, while humid Queensland is marginal. Queensland shows the most fig search impressions,
  so it still gets a first-class overlay. Fig was moved from the "subtropical" to the "mediterranean"
  climate category so its pages stop inheriting the stone-fruit chill-hours note.

### Verification

## Tamarillo growing guide (third enriched species)

Tamarillo (the tree tomato) now has the same rich, per-state, cited growing guide that olive and lychee
got. Tamarillo is a frost-tender, wind-sensitive, cool-subtropical plant, so its flagship state is New
South Wales (the widest frost-free, warm-temperate country), with genuinely different advice for
Queensland (the cooler elevated districts, not the humid tropics), Victoria (frost-free coastal and
bayside pockets) and Western Australia (Perth's frost-free coast, plus the usual quarantine note).

### Shipped
- growing_guides/tamarillo.json: 19 verified sources, a shared core (red versus gold types, self-fertile
  pollination, planting, water and feeding, the brittle-wood staking point, harvest by colour, eating
  without the bitter skin, buying tips) and four distinct state overlays.
- Archives first: grounded in Benedict's Rare Fruit Council archive articles on the tree tomato and the
  WANATCA Yearbook 21 article "Pruning Tamarillos" by Pat Sale, then cross-checked against CRFG, Morton
  (Purdue), the NZ Tree Crops Association, a peer-reviewed review, Business Queensland and DPIRD WA. The
  owned archive links are followed in Further reading.
- Honest where the evidence is thin: there is no NSW factsheet on tamarillo, so NSW is framed by climate
  rather than invented districts, and unverifiable pest and disease names were written in plain terms
  (root rot, powdery mildew) rather than overclaimed.

### Reality check
- At today's stock levels tamarillo does not yet reach the three-in-stock cut for a state buying page, so
  the new guide currently powers the species page and is ready to light up the state pages the moment
  stock rises (tamarillo is seasonal).

### Verification
- Full test suite 236 tests pass (added 14 tamarillo guards). All cited and Further reading URLs verified
  live (HTTP 200). No em or en dashes.

## Guava growing guide: Queensland flagship, cited and unique per state

The guava pages are the next species (after olive) to get a researched, per-state growing guide,
so buy-guava-trees-queensland, -new-south-wales, -victoria and -western-australia no longer share a
generic blurb. Guava is a natural pick: it is in stock across all four states, and as a tropical to
subtropical fruit its Australian home is Queensland, which is the flagship here.

### Shipped (PR open, awaiting Benedict's review)
- A new guava guide (tools/scrapers/growing_guides/guava.json) with a shared core (variety choice,
  pollination, planting, water, harvest, eating, buying) and a genuinely different overlay for each
  state: where guava grows, the harvest window, the pests that matter there, and a responsible note
  on strawberry guava as a coastal-weed risk.
- Each state names the right pest reality: Western Australia's fruit fly is the Mediterranean fruit
  fly (and Queensland fruit fly is not established there), while the eastern states battle Queensland
  fruit fly and, on the humid coast, myrtle rust.
- Further reading cross-links Benedict's own archives (a WANATCA conference paper and the Rare Fruit
  Council's guava articles) plus the WA Rare Fruit Club page (marked nofollow as a third party).

### Verification
- Full test suite 253 pass (a new 27-test guava file). Built against live stock: four pages unique
  per state, no em or en dashes, with FAQ structured data, article tags and cited sources. Every
  cited source and further-reading link checked live (HTTP 200).

### Worth recording
- The older species blurb claimed strawberry guava is "on the WA alert list". That is not correct:
  the WA Organism List lists both common and strawberry guava as permitted. Strawberry guava is an
  environmental weed in coastal Queensland and New South Wales, but it is not restricted in WA.
