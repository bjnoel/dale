# Public Ledger — 2026-06-02

## Olive guides now cite and cross-link Benedict's own horticultural archives

treestock's growing guides now draw on two archives Benedict hosts: WANATCA (the West Australian
Nut and Tree Crop Association, wanatca.org.au) and the Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives
(rfcarchives.org.au). Because these are owned domains, they work as first-party citations and
"Further reading" cross-links: better authority, no link rot, and readers stay within Benedict's
sites.

### Shipped
- The olive pages now cite Stan Kailis, "Growing Olives in Western Australia" (WANATCA Yearbook 22,
  1998), a WA-specific source by the recognised authority, and carry a Further reading block linking
  the Kailis article and the Rare Fruit Council's olive articles. Owned-site links are followed;
  third-party links are marked nofollow.
- A reusable index (build_archive_index.py, writing archive_links.json) maps the Rare Fruit
  Council's species-organised articles (about 30 species, including mango, custard apple, jaboticaba,
  fig and durian) to treestock species, so every future species guide auto-pulls relevant first-party
  cross-links. WANATCA yearbook articles are surfaced as a curation aid (their keyword matches are
  looser, so they are added by hand).

### Verification
- Full test suite 221 tests pass (added FurtherReadingTests and ArchiveIndexTests). Cited and linked
  URLs verified live (HTTP 200). No em or en dashes.

### Next
- Enrich the next species (mango, flagship Queensland) using the index. rarefruitclub.au (the WA Rare

## Guava growing guide: Queensland flagship, cited and unique per state

The guava pages are the next species (after olive) to get a researched, per-state growing guide,
so buy-guava-trees-queensland, -new-south-wales, -victoria and -western-australia no longer share a
generic blurb. Guava is a natural pick: it is in stock across all four states, and as a tropical to
subtropical fruit its Australian home is Queensland, which is the flagship here.

### Shipped (PR open, awaiting Benedict's review)
- A new guava guide (tools/scrapers/growing_guides/guava.json) with a shared core (variety choice,
  pollination, planting, water, harvest, eating, buying) and a genuinely different overlay for each
  state: where guava grows, the harvest window, the pests that matter there, and a responsible note
  on strawberry guava as a coastal-weed risk.
- Each state names the right pest reality: Western Australia's fruit fly is the Mediterranean fruit
  fly (and Queensland fruit fly is not established there), while the eastern states battle Queensland
  fruit fly and, on the humid coast, myrtle rust.
- Further reading cross-links Benedict's own archives (a WANATCA conference paper and the Rare Fruit
  Council's guava articles) plus the WA Rare Fruit Club page (marked nofollow as a third party).

### Verification
- Full test suite 253 pass (a new 27-test guava file). Built against live stock: four pages unique
  per state, no em or en dashes, with FAQ structured data, article tags and cited sources. Every
  cited source and further-reading link checked live (HTTP 200).

### Worth recording
- The older species blurb claimed strawberry guava is "on the WA alert list". That is not correct:
  the WA Organism List lists both common and strawberry guava as permitted. Strawberry guava is an
  environmental weed in coastal Queensland and New South Wales, but it is not restricted in WA.
