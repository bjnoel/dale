# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Lilly pilly gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Lilly pilly is one of the most planted plants in Australian gardens, grown two ways at once: as a fast, dense evergreen hedge and for its small edible berries, the best of which (the riberry) is a genuine bush food. It is also one of treestock's best-stocked species, with dozens of named cultivars across the whole group (Syzygium australe, the former Acmena now called Syzygium smithii, the riberry, the magenta and blue lilly pillies and more). Until now its pages carried a single generic blurb. This change gives lilly pilly the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, plus a much richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)

- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a variety for a hedge, the best fruit, or both; the all-important resistance to the pimple psyllid; why one self-fertile plant will fruit; planting and soil; water and feeding; pruning and hedging; harvest and eating; and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. New South Wales leads, as the native heartland of the whole group, home to the rare magenta lilly pilly found nowhere else, and the birthplace of the riberry bush-food trade. Queensland covers the fastest, densest hedges in the country and the heaviest psyllid and myrtle rust pressure. Victoria covers the hardy, frost-tolerant hedge plant that reaches its wild southern limit on Wilsons Promontory. Western Australia covers an introduced ornamental that, for now, escapes the myrtle rust troubling the east.
- Lilly pilly gets its own climate category rather than the generic "subtropical" note, which was wrong for it twice over: it implied a frost-tender plant that is marginal in the cool south (lilly pilly is hardy and the default hedge plant across Victoria), and it implied the usual eastern-nursery shipping story for Western Australia. The truth is the opposite of free: lilly pilly is a myrtle, and WA restricts myrtle-family plants to keep out myrtle rust, so live plants essentially cannot be posted in from interstate and WA buyers rely on locally grown stock.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own WANATCA and Rare Fruit Council archives (including Peter Wilson's "Syzygium as a source of edible fruits" from WANATCA Yearbook 14), then cross-checked against DPIRD Western Australia, Business Queensland, the Commonwealth DCCEEW, DBCA, the herbaria (VicFlora and the Atlas of Living Australia), the Australian Native Plants Society and the Australian Plants Society, the Australian Flora Foundation and AgriFutures.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: psyllid resistance is the single most important thing to check when buying (choose a former Acmena or riberry, or a named resistant brush cherry); every lilly pilly berry is edible and none are poisonous; one plant fruits on its own; lilly pillies are in the myrtle family, not the protea family, so they are not phosphorus-sensitive and take an ordinary fertiliser; myrtle rust is the headline disease of the family along the east coast; and the fruit fly to plan around is the Queensland fruit fly in the east (a recorded host of the berries) while Western Australia's Mediterranean fruit fly does not list lilly pilly as a host at all.
