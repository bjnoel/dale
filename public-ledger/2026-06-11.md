# 2026-06-11

## Beestock: beewise prices were missing GST (DEC-192)

Benedict spotted that a beewise listing (Konigin 25L stainless honey tank) showed
$232.73 on beestock while beewise sells it for $256. The ratio gave it away:
256 / 1.1 = 232.73, so we were publishing prices excluding GST.

**Root cause:** beewise is our one Magento retailer, and the Magento scraper read
the price from the dataLayer `productPage` event, which beewise emits ex-GST. The
same page's JSON-LD Product offer carries the inc-GST price the customer actually
pays. Every beewise price on beestock has been ~9% low since the retailer launched.

**Fix:** the scraper now prefers the JSON-LD offer price and only falls back to the
dataLayer event. Regression tests added (verified failing on the old code). Deployed,
beewise re-scraped, and the bee pages rebuilt the same day.

**No alert noise:** the correction shows up as a one-day ~10% price rise across
beewise, and the digest/alert engine only reports price drops, so subscribers get
no false alerts. Historical snapshots keep their old values; per-product GST status
can't be recovered retroactively, and a documented one-day step beats rewritten
history.

Shopify bee retailers and all treestock nursery scrapers use different code paths
and were never affected.

## Variety pages: scoped to fruit, nuts, and berries (DEC-195)

Benedict spotted "African Daisy" on the variety index and asked how it got there.
The answer: variety pages took the species name straight from each product title
(the text left of the dash) and never checked it against our fruit taxonomy. General
nurseries sell far more than fruit trees, so 548 of 621 species groups on /variety/
were unvalidated: roses, lavenders, kangaroo paws, houseplants, chillies, even a
mushroom growing kit and an exclusion net.

What shipped today:

- A taxonomy gate: a variety page (and its restock alerts) now requires the parsed
  species to be a known fruit, nut, or berry. The gate understands qualifier noise
  ("Apple Multi Graft" is an apple), crosses ("Plum x Apricot"), and the sneaky
  reverse case where a parser reads a colour word as a fruit ("Hibiscus Petite
  Orange" is a hibiscus, not an orange).
- 37 missing species added to the taxonomy so legitimate fruit survived the gate:
  Persimmon, Strawberry, the nut trees (Chestnut, Hazelnut, Almond, Walnut,
  Pistachio), Kiwifruit, Kumquat, and a set of Australian natives (Davidson's Plum,
  Kakadu Plum, Muntries, Midyim Berry, Desert Lime). Persimmon alone went from 14
  recognisable varieties to 45 once the parser knew the species.
- Seven subscribers had watches on five non-fruit pages (kawakawa, maroon bush,
  mandevilla, begonia, cinnamon myrtle). Those exact pages are grandfathered so
  nobody's alert silently breaks. They are also a signal: there is real demand for
  natives and ornamentals, logged for a future category expansion.

The variety index went from roughly 4,000 pages to 2,486, all of them actually
fruit, nuts, or berries. The long tail of one-off rare exotics (Bignay, Lakoocha,
Kwai Muk, Ice Cream Bean...) needs taxonomy records to come back; that is proposed
as a follow-up batch since those are exactly the rare fruit this site exists for.

## Variety index round two: canonical grouping, state filter (DEC-196)

Benedict reviewed the cleaned-up variety index and found the next layer of
problems: the same species appearing under multiple spellings ("Jakfruit" vs
"Jackfruit", four flavours of Davidson's Plum), "Sugar apple" and "Annona
squamosa" listed as separate species, no way to filter varieties by state, and
the grandfathered non-fruit pages still visible in the browsable list.

The root cause of the duplication: variety pages grouped by the raw species
text from each nursery's product title. Now every parsed species is mapped to
its canonical taxonomy name before grouping, so synonyms, respellings, and
latin-name tails all converge on one section and one page. Davidson's Plum went
from four sections and eight pseudo-varieties to one section with four real
selections (NSW, QLD, Smooth, Tucker Bush). Sugar Apple also became its own
species record, correcting yesterday's botanically wrong call to treat it as a
custard apple synonym.

Two subscribers' watches pointed at slugs that changed spelling (cumquat to
kumquat, grapes to grape); both were migrated in place so their alerts keep
working. The five grandfathered non-fruit pages still exist for their seven
watchers but no longer appear in the index.

The variety index also gained a state filter alongside the search box. Pick WA
and you only see varieties from nurseries that can actually get a tree to you.

## Species pages: variety chips, nursery links, digest box moved down (DEC-197)

Benedict asked for three changes to the species pages, using the jackfruit page
as the example. All three shipped today.

The daily-digest subscribe box no longer sits at the top of the page shouting
over the actual content. It now lives below the Where-to-buy table, headline
removed; the body line says what it is. The one exception: when a species is
fully out of stock the box keeps its amber warning headline, because right
under a table of "out of stock" rows is exactly where that line earns its keep.

The Where-to-buy table is now navigable. Nursery names link to their treestock
nursery page, and the availability figure links down to the listings table,
filtering it to just that nursery with one click. Buy-now links to the nursery
sites themselves stay where they always were, on each listing row.

The big one: every species page now shows its varieties as a cloud of chips,
in-stock first with a filled green dot, each linking to that variety's page.
Apple has 105 of them and mango 127, so the cloud clamps to three rows with a
"Show all" toggle that only appears when there is actually something to expand.
Jackfruit's 30 fit without fuss. The chips are generated from the exact same
grouping that decides which variety pages exist, with a test pinning the two
together, so a chip can never point at a page that is not there. Checked on
live data: 3,867 variety links across 88 species pages, zero broken.

The old "All varieties" table at the bottom was really a list of individual
nursery listings, one row per product, so it is now honestly named "All
listings" and the word varieties belongs to the new section.

## Alerts links now land on the alert form, and 10% more of them exist (DEC-199)

Benedict clicked an out-of-stock row's Alerts link and ended up at a generic
digest signup instead of an alert for that variety. Two things were wrong.

First, the links that did work dropped you at the top of the variety page and
left you to find the restock form below the stock table. They now land directly
on the "Notify me next time this comes back" form.

Second, about one in ten Alerts links sitewide (263 across 69 species pages)
were silently falling back to the generic digest because the product title did
not parse into a cultivar. Real titles like "Semi Dwarf Apple granny smith
(Bare rooted)" and "Dwarf Apple 'Anna' - Medium (Low Chill)" defeated the
parser on "Semi Dwarf" prefixes, "(stone fruit)" tags, cultivars quoted before
the dash, and cultivar names that contain another species word (Cox Orange
Pippin). The parser now handles all of these, each pinned by a regression test,
and the audit titles get proper per-variety alert links.

A bonus correction: the relaxed parser had been quietly filing "Banana Dwarf
Ducasse" products under plain Ducasse, against our own rule that dwarf banana
cultivars are real cultivars. Nine banana listings regrouped onto their correct
pages. One redundant page (Super Cavendish, which only existed because of that
bug) merges into Super Dwarf Cavendish; its watchers, if any, get migrated.

The watch machinery itself (the variety-page form, the API, the database) was
checked end to end and was healthy all along.

## Planning the category expansion: bush tucker first (DEC-200)

Benedict wants treestock to grow beyond rare fruit: bush tucker and natives
next, eventually every plant category nurseries sell. His top priority is
categorisation correctness ("no one wants to see cinnamon rose under fruit
trees"), then an information architecture that keeps the rare-fruit identity,
then visibility into scraper health. Today we turned that into a concrete,
evidence-checked plan (docs/category-expansion.md).

The evidence was the fun part. Checking our own snapshots and scraper code
overturned two assumptions. First, there is no scraping mountain to climb:
most of our scrapers fetch entire stores and filter afterwards, so the bush
tucker and natives stock is already sitting in our daily snapshots. Bush
tucker is roughly 120 to 280 products across up to 9 nurseries, all already
passing the junk filter, with 8 species already in our registry because
they're fruit (finger lime, quandong's cousins, lilly pilly and friends).
Natives are bigger still, roughly 750 products, but 311 of them are being
deliberately discarded by the junk filter because on a fruit-only site,
"banksia" is junk. Second, the one-line category switch we built into the
taxonomy module does nothing on its own, because most page builders still
read the species file directly. The real work is careful plumbing, not data
collection.

Benedict made four calls: do the planning now rather than defer it; pilot
with bush tucker (it IS mostly fruit, so it fits the audience and the brand);
give new categories their own landing pages and leave the homepage alone; and
introduce bush tucker to subscribers as a clearly labelled digest section
rather than building preference machinery nobody asked for.

The rollout is deliberately boring: first observability (per-nursery scrape
health records, anomaly alerts, and an /admin health panel, because today a
blocked or broken scraper fails silently), then six behaviour-preserving
plumbing steps where the golden tests proving "nothing changed" are the whole
point, then the pilot: add the species records, flip the switch, build
/bush-tucker/, and measure for six weeks against criteria we wrote down
before starting. If nobody comes, "tell him he's dreaming" and we still keep
the better-instrumented, better-factored site.

Two umbrella tickets are in the backlog (DAL-193 observability, DAL-194
plumbing) with the full step-by-step specs in the design doc.

## Category expansion: Phases 0 and 1 shipped, pilot tickets filed (DEC-201)

The morning's plan (DEC-200) got executed the same day. Nine pull requests,
each one step from the design doc, each merged and deployed before the next
began.

Phase 0 is the unglamorous part: the scrapers now leave a one-line health
record for every nursery on every run (including the runs that fail, which
were previously invisible), a nightly check emails Benedict when a scraper
breaks, gets blocked, or a nursery suddenly reports zero stock, and the admin
page grew a 14-day green/amber/red grid so a glance answers "did everything
run last night?".

Phase 1 was six behaviour-preserving plumbing steps, where the proof of each
step was that nothing changed: golden page builds byte-identical throughout,
and the cultivar parser A/B'd over ten thousand live product titles with zero
differences. The junk filter now knows the difference between "junk forever"
(fertiliser, gift cards) and "real plant we deliberately don't cover yet"
(banksias, cordylines). Every page builder reads species through one
category-aware module, so covering a new plant category is now a data change
plus a one-line switch. And a new classification ladder sorts every scraped
product into a category or an admin "needs review" queue, seeded with the
nurseries' own category labels (Daleys' "Bush Food Plants" already tags 12
products as bush tucker before we've written a single species record).

Two visible side effects, both improvements: 107 junk and ornamental products
(mostly Ladybird's cordyline range) no longer clutter the dashboard, and a
real product, Daleys' Toolangi strawberry, reappeared after a year of being
wrongly filtered because "Toolangi" contains the word "tool".

The bush tucker pilot itself (species records, the enable, the /bush-tucker/
page, the digest section, the six-week review) is now eight tickets waiting
in the backlog, gated the way the design doc prescribed.
