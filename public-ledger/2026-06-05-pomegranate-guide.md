# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Pomegranate growing guide: a dry-climate fruit, told honestly per state

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for pomegranates (Punica granatum). Like the
others, it replaces the generic blurb on /species/pomegranate.html and on the buy-pomegranate-trees-[state]
pages with scannable, cited, state-aware advice. Pomegranates are one of the oldest cultivated fruits
and one of the most drought-tolerant trees a collector can grow, but they are fussy about one thing
above all, and getting that right is what the guide is built around.

That one thing is fruit splitting. A pomegranate is a hot-dry-summer plant, and its fruit cracks open
when a tree that has been short of water suddenly gets a lot of it, from rain or heavy irrigation, as
the fruit nears ripeness. So the guide leads, in the core and in every state, with even watering, mulch,
and picking promptly ahead of autumn rain. The flip side is that pomegranates do not ripen after they
are picked, so they have to come off the tree fully ripe (deep red, flattened, with a metallic ring
when tapped), which the guide spells out so nobody picks early and waits in vain for fruit to sweeten
on the bench.

### Things the guide gets right that the generic copy did not
- One tree is enough. Pomegranates are self-fruitful, so a single plant crops on its own; a second of
  any variety only lifts the set further. No one needs to be talked into buying a pollinator partner.
- The fruit fly to plan around depends on where you are. In Western Australia it is the Mediterranean
  fruit fly, and a pomegranate's thick, leathery rind keeps it off the state's host list unless the
  fruit has already split. In the eastern states it is the Queensland fruit fly, which does list
  pomegranate as a host.
- Humidity, not cold, is the real enemy. Along the humid Queensland and northern New South Wales coast
  the fruit splits and rots and colours poorly, so the guide sends growers to the hot, dry inland and
  the cooler, drier elevated districts instead.
- Variety advice is tied to what is actually in the nurseries (Wonderful, Gulosha Rosavaya, Azerbaijani,
  Midnight Velvet, Red Velvet, Elche, Parfianka, Veles), with soft-seeded types flagged for fresh
  eating and harder-seeded ones for juice.

### Built on first-party and authoritative sources
Western Australia is the flagship state, and its overlay rests on Benedict's own archives: an
Agriculture WA cultivar trial at the Medina research station, which rated Gulosha Rosavaya the best
all-rounder and Wonderful next; a grower's account of an orchard at Yarloop; and a WANATCA conference
paper on establishing an orchard at Yallingup. The rest of the guide leans on AgriFutures, a City of
Darwin fact sheet, the Business Queensland and DPIRD biosecurity pages, and university tree-fruit
extension. Every claim is cited and every link (24 in all) was checked to load before shipping.
