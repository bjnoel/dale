# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Loquat gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Loquat is one of the easiest and most rewarding backyard fruit trees in Australia, a handsome evergreen that flowers in autumn and ripens one of the first crops of the year, in late winter and spring, well ahead of almost everything else. treestock tracks it across several nurseries (Bessell Brown, Nagasakiwase, Champagne and others), but until now its page carried a single generic blurb. This change gives loquat the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a much richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)

- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a named variety, why one self-fertile tree will fruit, planting and soil, water and feeding, thinning each cluster for size, pruning and tree size, harvest and handling, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Western Australia leads, because Perth's mild, near frost-free winters suit loquat's unusual autumn-to-winter flowering, it is widely grown in Perth gardens, and it is the only loquat buy-page that earns search traffic today. Queensland explains why loquat fruits well in the cooler subtropical south and the uplands but grows only as an ornamental in the humid tropical lowlands. New South Wales covers the classic Sydney backyard tree and the frost risk on the cold tablelands. Victoria covers the hardy, widely-grown Melbourne tree that simply ripens latest of the mainland states.
- Loquat gets its own climate category rather than the generic "subtropical" note, which was wrong for it twice over: it implied a frost-tender tree that is marginal in the cool south (the tree is hardy and a common sight in Melbourne gardens) and it implied the usual Western Australian quarantine wall (loquat is a permitted plant in WA with no loquat-specific restriction, so it ships in more freely than most fruit trees).
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council and WANATCA archives (including C.A. Schroeder's "The Loquat: A Fruit of Quality" from WANATCA Yearbook 20, and Neville Passmore's "Exotic Fruits in Perth"), then cross-checked against DPIRD Western Australia, Business Queensland, the California Rare Fruit Growers and Clemson factsheets, Brisbane City Council, Yates and Bunnings.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: one self-fertile tree will fruit (a second variety only improves the crop); the fruit must be picked fully ripe because it does not sweeten after picking; thinning each cluster to a few fruit is the single job that turns a disappointing loquat into an excellent one; the large seeds are not for eating; and the fruit fly to plan around is the Mediterranean fruit fly in WA (loquat is a high-susceptibility host) and the Queensland fruit fly in the east, with the winter-ripening crop helping carry fruit fly through the cold season.
