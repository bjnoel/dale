# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Jujube gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Jujube (Ziziphus jujuba, also called Chinese date or red date) is one of the toughest, most collector-worthy fruit trees we track, and treestock carries an unusually deep range of it (around 80 listings across five nurseries). Until now its pages shared a single generic blurb. This change gives jujube the same treatment as olive and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, plus a richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a fresh-eating versus drying variety, pollination, planting and soil, water and feeding, the all-important thorns-suckers-and-rootstock section, harvest fresh and dried, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Western Australia leads, because WA is, with South Australia, one of Australia's two leading jujube-producing states; Queensland covers the warm dry inland around Goondiwindi and the Granite Belt, not the humid coast; New South Wales covers the warm inland (the Central West and Riverina) rather than the coast; and Victoria covers the Sunraysia heartland around Mildura.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council and WANATCA archives (Roger Meyer's "Jujube Orchards", Phil Ciminata's Perth-authored "The Chinese Jujube" from the 1996 yearbook, and more), then cross-checked against New Mexico State University and California extension guides, DPIRD WA, Business Queensland, the national biosecurity assessment, the NT Government and Australian jujube growers.
- Gets the naming right, which trips a lot of buyers: the deciduous, temperate Chinese jujube is kept clearly distinct from the evergreen tropical Indian jujube (Ziziphus mauritiana), a different species with different needs, and the Indian-jujube archive article is deliberately kept out of the further reading.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: jujube is largely self-fertile but crops better with a second variety; it is precocious, exceptionally drought and heat hardy, and tolerates poor, alkaline and salty soils, but wants a hot dry summer and dislikes humid coasts; its root suckers are wild rootstock, not the grafted variety, and need controlling; in WA the fruit fly to net against is the Mediterranean fruit fly (jujube is a recorded host there, and WA has no established Queensland fruit fly), while in the east the Chinese jujube is not on the official Queensland fruit fly host list but is still worth netting.
- Corrects the old assumption that jujube faces no real WA quarantine: WA does regulate interstate live plants, so the guide explains the bare-rooted, soil-free, certified path honestly while pointing growers to the WA-grown stock that makes WA an easy place to buy.
