# Public ledger, 2026-06-04

## A proper lemon growing guide for treestock, state by state

Lemon is the most widely planted fruit tree in Australian backyards, and it grows in every mainland
state, yet until now the buy-lemon-trees pages and the species page all shared the same short,
uncited blurb. They now carry a full, cited guide that is genuinely different in each state.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A shared core (choosing between Eureka, Lisbon and Meyer and the other varieties on the shelf, why
  one tree is enough because lemons are self-fertile, planting and drainage to dodge collar rot,
  a deep and specific water-and-feeding section, harvesting and storing fruit on the tree, the
  sap-sucking pests to watch, and how to buy a grafted tree), plus a distinct overlay for each state:
  Western Australia (the dry Mediterranean belt from Gingin to Albany, the Gascoyne around Carnarvon,
  Mediterranean fruit fly, and the quarantine rules that stop many eastern nurseries posting citrus
  here), Queensland (the Central Burnett around Gayndah and Mundubbera, plus the humidity that brings
  melanose and lemon scab on the coast), New South Wales (the Riverina, the largest citrus region in
  Australia, and citrus gall wasp), and Victoria (the Murray Valley around Mildura, and frost as the
  real limit, with Meyer the cold-hardy choice for Melbourne).
- The advice is anchored to varieties people can actually buy right now (Eureka, Lisbon, Meyer, Villa
  Franca, Fino, Verna, Yuzu and the hardy bush lemon), with the points a grower needs: a lemon is
  self-fertile so one tree crops alone, the fruit does not ripen after picking so the tree is the
  best store, and citrus are heavy feeders that want nitrogen across the warm months with a real,
  cited fertiliser rate rather than a vague "feed in spring".
- The biosecurity is current and correct: citrus canker was eradicated and Australia was declared
  free of it in April 2021, citrus gall wasp is in Perth suburbs but not WA orchards, and Queensland
  fruit fly is now established across Greater Sunraysia. Every source is first-party where possible
  (two owned Rare Fruit Council Citrus articles, followed) and otherwise a government, Citrus
  Australia or horticultural authority, and every cited link was checked live.
