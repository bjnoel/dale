## Orange growing guide: the backyard favourite, state by state

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for the orange (Citrus sinensis), the most
popular citrus in the Australian backyard. Like the others, it replaces the generic blurb on
/species/orange.html and on the buy-orange-trees-[state] pages with scannable, cited, state-aware
advice. Oranges grow somewhere in every state, so unlike the rare tropicals, all four state pages
(Western Australia, Queensland, New South Wales and Victoria) are live straight away.

New South Wales is the flagship, because the Riverina around Griffith and Leeton is the single
largest citrus district in the country and grows more oranges than anywhere else, so it was
researched the deepest. Every other state still gets a genuinely different overlay: Victoria leads on
its cool-night blood-orange advantage and the Sunraysia navel country around Mildura, Queensland on
the Central Burnett heartland around Gayndah and Mundubbera, and Western Australia on its
Mediterranean south-west, fruit fly and quarantine.

### Researched archives-first, and two things the guide gets right
The guide is built on Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council and WANATCA archives first (the citrus
rootstock article, the piece on how climate shapes citrus colour and flavour, and the blood-orange
article), then cross-checked against Citrus Australia, the state agriculture departments and
university horticulture extension. Two facts the research corrected, each now locked in by a test:

- Queensland's citrus canker story is told correctly. The Emerald outbreak was in 2004 (eradicated
  after hundreds of thousands of trees were removed), and Australia was declared free of citrus
  canker in 2021. The guide does not repeat the common mix-up that pins a 2018 outbreak on Emerald
  (the 2018 incursion was in Darwin and northern WA).
- Western Australia is not described as free of citrus gall wasp. It has been in Perth backyards
  since 2013, but it has stayed out of WA's commercial orchards and country districts, and the real
  WA citrus pest is Mediterranean fruit fly, not the Queensland fruit fly of the eastern states. The
  guide says exactly that, so a Perth grower gets honest advice.

### What is in it
- One state-invariant core: choosing between navel, Valencia, blood and sour oranges; why every
  orange is grafted and what the rootstock does; the good news that one tree is enough (oranges are
  self fertile and navels are seedless); planting, soil and frost; deep, cited watering and feeding;
  harvesting and the orange's trick of storing on the tree; eating versus juicing; and buying tips.
- Four genuinely different state overlays (climate fit, the real growing regions, harvest windows
  and the pests where you are), plus net-new questions for each state, from keeping fruit fly off a
  Perth tree to why blood oranges colour so well in a cool Victorian winter.
