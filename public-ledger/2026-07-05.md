# 2026-07-05

## Traffic focus: bare-root season page + variety excerpts on species pages (DEC-213)

Benedict asked what to focus on next for treestock traffic. Pulled 90 days of Plausible and 28 days of Search Console: the organic base has grown from ~350 to ~550-690 visitors/week, driven almost entirely by Google long-tail into species pages and guides. The blockers are clear: the highest-impression species pages sit at positions 15-35, and the queries reaching them are mostly variety-specific.

Shipped today:

- **/bare-root.html**: a season-aware landing page for the June-September bare-root window (we are mid-season now). What bare-root means, which species and nurseries sell it, and a live table of every bare-root tree in stock across the tracked nurseries, cheapest first, rebuilt nightly. Out of season the page stays live and honest ("season closed") with a subscribe prompt for next year.
- **Notable varieties on species pages**: treestock has 1,153 hand-verified variety descriptions but they only rendered on the variety pages. Species pages now surface one-line excerpts for the top in-stock described varieties, adding unique content that matches how people actually search ("dwarf pakistan mulberry tree for sale").
- Consolidated three stale fortnightly GSC review tickets into one striking-distance worklist (DAL-208).

Also shipped today:

- **Growing-guide content** for the biggest search-opportunity queries: a dwarf-olive-Perth answer and a mature-olive-Victoria answer (feeding the olive buy-by-state pages), and a "can you buy Sumo mandarin trees in Australia?" explainer (Sumo is the Shiranui/Dekopon variety, which several tracked nurseries sell).
- **Fruit tree pollination guide** (/fruit-tree-pollination-guide.html): a new flagship guide answering the question that trips up most fruit-tree buyers, do you need two trees? It grades every claim by evidence quality and covers apple, pear, cherry, plum, avocado (Type A and B), kiwifruit, pistachio, blueberry and the self-fertile fruits, with sources verified against Australian and international horticultural authorities (BeeAware, WSU, UC, Clemson, and established Australian nurseries).
