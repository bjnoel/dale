# Public Ledger, 2026-06-04

## Cherry growing guide: the hardest backyard stone fruit, told honestly per state

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for cherries (Prunus avium). Like the others, it
replaces the generic blurb on /species/cherry.html and on the buy-cherry-trees-[state] pages with
scannable, cited, state-aware advice. Cherries are the showpiece of the summer fruit bowl and the
single hardest of the common stone fruits to grow at home, so getting the advice right matters: the
wrong variety or the wrong district wastes a grower years.

The guide is built around the one fact that decides everything with cherries: winter chill. Most
sweet cherries need a long, cold winter (roughly 800 to 1200 hours below 7 degrees), far more than an
apple or a peach. That single requirement is why cherries thrive in the cold tablelands of New South
Wales and the high country of Victoria, struggle almost everywhere in Western Australia, and grow in
just one cold corner of Queensland, the Granite Belt. Because the old, shared blurb told growers to
"choose low-chill varieties" (sound advice for apples, wrong for cherries), cherry now has its own
climate note in each state that tells the real cold-climate story.

### Things the guide gets right that the generic copy did not
- The low-chill option is real but specific. A Californian-bred series (Minnie Royal, Royal Lee and
  Royal Crimson) needs only about 250 hours of chill and has made cherries possible in milder
  districts. The catch, which the guide spells out, is that Minnie Royal and Royal Lee have to be
  grown together because each one pollinates the other.
- Pollination is the other make-or-break. Most sweet cherries cannot set fruit with their own pollen
  and need a compatible partner flowering at the same time, with bees to do the work. The guide names
  the self-fertile varieties that crop alone (Stella, Lapins, Sunburst and others) so a one-tree
  garden can still get fruit.
- Prune in summer, not winter. Cutting a cherry in the cold, wet of winter opens the door to silver
  leaf and bacterial canker, two diseases that kill limbs, so the guide follows the orchard rule of
  pruning in summer or straight after harvest.
- Cherries do not ripen after picking. They are non-climacteric, so the guide tells you to leave them
  on the tree until they are fully coloured and sweet, then pick with the stalk on.
- In Western Australia the fruit fly to plan around is the Mediterranean fruit fly, not the Queensland
  fruit fly that the eastern states manage, and WA quarantine tightly controls cherry (a stone fruit)
  nursery stock, which is why many eastern nurseries will not post a cherry tree west.

### Built on first-party and authoritative sources
Every claim is cited, and every link was checked to load before shipping (33 in all). The owned
"Further reading" is a WANATCA Yearbook article, "The True Cherries", from Benedict's own archive.
The rest lean on Cherry Growers Australia, the National Cherry Festival at Young, Batlow, the Yarra
Valley and Granite Belt growers, DPIRD in Western Australia, Business Queensland, and university tree
fruit research, rather than the government pages that block automated checks.
