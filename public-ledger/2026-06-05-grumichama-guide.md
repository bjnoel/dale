# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Grumichama (Brazil cherry) gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Grumichama, also called the Brazil cherry, is a slow, handsome Brazilian rainforest tree that bears small, sweet, cherry-like fruit. treestock tracks it across Daleys, Ross Creek Tropicals, Fruitopia, Ladybird and Primal Fruits, in black, yellow (or orange) and compact dwarf forms. Until now its page carried a single generic blurb. This change gives grumichama the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing between the black, yellow and dwarf forms, the pollination story, planting and acid soil, water and feeding, harvest and ripening, eating and using, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because warm, humid coastal Queensland (the subtropical south east up to the tropical far north, where the rare-fruit branches around Ingham and Cardwell grew it for decades) is the real Australian home of the grumichama. Western Australia covers the frost-light Perth coast and the warm Gascoyne, with steady summer water the key in the dry heat. New South Wales covers the Northern Rivers heartland and how the tree's cold-hardiness lets it reach sheltered Sydney gardens. Victoria covers it as the hardiest of the tropical cherries, achievable in a warm Melbourne microclimate or a pot.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council archives (four grumichama articles, including Gene Joyner's and the Ingham Branch account), then Daleys, the Useful Tropical Plants database and a Cairns tropical-gardening grower for Australian specifics, and cross-checked against Business Queensland, the federal agriculture department, DCCEEW and DPIRD WA for fruit fly, myrtle rust and Western Australian quarantine.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower. Grumichama is self-fertile, so one tree will fruit on its own. It is cold-hardier than most subtropical fruit, with established trees taking a light frost of about minus 3 degrees, which is why it grows further south than its cousins. The crop is fast and short, ripening only about four to five weeks after a spring flowering, all at once in the subtropics and in several flushes a year in the tropics. And, unlike its thick-skinned relative the jaboticaba, the grumichama has thin, soft skin, so it is a fruit-fly target (Queensland fruit fly in the eastern states, Mediterranean fruit fly in WA) and is best netted or baited. Birds love it too.
