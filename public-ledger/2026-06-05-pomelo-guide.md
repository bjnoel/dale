# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Pomelo growing guide: the biggest citrus, and the parent of the grapefruit

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for pomelo (also spelled pummelo, Citrus maxima).
Like the others, it replaces the generic blurb on /species/pomelo.html and on the buy-pomelo-trees-[state]
pages with scannable, cited, state-aware advice. Pomelo is the largest of all the citrus, and the guide
is built around the two things that make it different from every other citrus on the site.

The first is that the relationship most people assume is backwards. A pomelo is not a big grapefruit. It
is the other way around: the pomelo came first, and the grapefruit was bred from it, as a cross between
pomelo and sweet orange. A good pomelo is bigger, sweeter and far less bitter than grapefruit, with a
thick pith that peels away so the segments lift out and eat by hand like an easy peel orange.

The second is climate. Pomelo sits at both extremes of the citrus range at once. It is the most heat and
humidity tolerant of the citrus, which is why it thrives on the warm, humid Queensland coast where other
citrus can struggle, and it is also the most frost tender, which is why it is a marginal, pot and shelter
crop in cool Victoria. The old generic blurb, and even the generic "citrus" treatment, missed both points,
so pomelo now has its own climate category and each state note tells its real story.

### Things the guide gets right that the generic copy did not
- Pollination depends on the variety. Most of the named pomelos sold here, including the seedless Vietnamese
  Nam Roi and the red fleshed Carter's Red, crop on their own, but pomelo as a species is variable and some
  traditional Asian varieties are self incompatible and need a different citrus nearby. Cross pollination
  also adds seeds, so a lone seedless variety stays seedless while the same tree in a busy mixed citrus
  patch carries more pips.
- Pomelo carries the grapefruit medication interaction. It contains the same furanocoumarin compounds, so
  the guide flags that anyone on regular prescription medicine should check with a pharmacist or doctor
  before eating a lot of it. Being sweeter does not make it safer in this respect.
- The fruit does not ripen after picking, so it has to come off the tree when it actually tastes sweet, and
  it stores on the tree for months, which the guide spells out so nobody picks early.
- Variety advice is tied to what is actually in the nurseries (Carter's Red, Nam Roi, Flicks Yellow, Thai
  Gold, K15, Thai Sun, Red Rouge, Tahitian and Chandler), split by flesh colour, red and pink for sweet
  eating and pale or seedless types for those who would rather skip the seeds.
- It explains why so many pomelo varieties are sold "Queensland only". Citrus is a fruit fly and citrus
  canker host, so live citrus plants are under interstate movement controls, and the specialist Queensland
  nurseries that hold the best range sell those trees for the eastern states only.

### Built on first-party and authoritative sources
Queensland is the flagship state. The guide is sourced archives first from Benedict's own collections: the
Rare Fruit Council archive article "The Pummelo", the citrus rootstocks article, and the citrus family
overview, plus two WANATCA pieces (David Karp's "Pummelos in California" yearbook article and a conference
paper on citrus in the tropics). The rest leans on Citrus Australia, DPIRD Western Australia, Business
Queensland, WA Citrus, university tree fruit extension, and peer reviewed papers for the pollination and
medication points. Every claim is cited and every link, 47 in all, was checked to load before shipping.
