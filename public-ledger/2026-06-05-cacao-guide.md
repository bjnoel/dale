# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Cacao (the chocolate tree) gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Cacao, Theobroma cacao, is the tree whose fermented beans become chocolate. treestock tracks it across several nurseries (Daleys, Fruitopia, Ross Creek Tropicals and Ladybird), sold mostly as seedlings or as the compact SG2 selection. Until now its page carried a single generic blurb. This change gives cacao the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, plus a much richer species page. It is the most climate-restricted species we have written up, so the guide is honest about that.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing between Criollo, Forastero and the Trinitario type grown here, the strange cauliflory and midge-pollination story behind its very low fruit set, planting under shade with wind shelter, water and feeding, harvest, and the all-important fermentation step that turns flavourless fresh beans into chocolate) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because the wet tropical far north (roughly Cardwell to the Daintree, with plantings around Mossman and Innisfail and a research station at South Johnstone) is the only part of Australia warm and humid enough to crop cacao, and the small Australian cocoa industry is centred there. Western Australia gets the deepest secondary overlay, built on the real, discontinued field trials at Broome and Kununurra, because even WA's tropical north could not crop it and Perth is far outside its range. New South Wales and Victoria cover keeping it alive under glass, because cacao will not take a cold winter outdoors anywhere in either state.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council and WANATCA archives (the Rare Fruit Council processing-cocoa fermentation article and the WANATCA Quandong cacao issue), then built on the authoritative Australian record: the eight-year RIRDC "Producing Cocoa in Northern Australia" research report, the Northern Territory Government, Business Queensland, AgriFutures, the International Cocoa Organisation, and the US National Park Service on the tiny midge that pollinates cocoa. The WA-specific detail is cross-linked (nofollow) to the Rare Fruit Club WA.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower. Cacao flowers straight from cushions on its trunk, and those flowers are pollinated not by bees but by midges no bigger than pinheads, so even a healthy tree sets pods on only one to five percent of its flowers. A split pod is not chocolate: the sweet white pulp is good to eat fresh, but the beans have no chocolate flavour until they are fermented for about a week and then dried. And there is a real biosecurity good-news story here: Australia is free of the witches broom, frosty pod and swollen shoot diseases that cripple cocoa overseas, and it eradicated the cocoa pod borer found in the far north in 2011 by 2014. For the cooler states, the guide points readers to the hardier black sapote (the chocolate pudding fruit) and to feijoa and tamarillo instead.
