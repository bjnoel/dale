# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Miracle fruit gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Miracle fruit (Synsepalum dulcificum) is one of the strangest plants a rare-fruit collector can own: its little red berry coats your tongue with a protein called miraculin and, for about an hour afterwards, makes anything sour taste sweet, so a straight lemon becomes a lolly. treestock tracks it across several nurseries (Daleys, Ross Creek Tropicals, Ladybird and others), but until now its page carried only a generic blurb. This change gives miracle fruit the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a much richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)

- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (the taste-changing berry and how to use it, why it is sold as seedlings rather than named varieties and fruits happily as a single plant, acid soil as the absolute make-or-break, warmth and humidity and shelter, water and feeding, harvest and eating, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because the humid tropical north is the one part of Australia where this West African shrub grows and fruits outdoors much as it does at home, and where it was first fruited here in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Western Australia explains the real local challenge, which is not cold but chemistry: Perth's alkaline sandy soils and hard, limey scheme water fight the plant's need for strongly acid conditions, so in WA it is almost always a pot plant. New South Wales splits into the warm, near frost-free far north coast (outdoors) and everywhere else (a potted shrub brought under cover for winter). Victoria is honest that it is an indoor or heated-glasshouse plant there, grown for novelty more than crops.

- Miracle fruit gets its own climate category rather than the generic "tropical" note, which was misleading: it implied a heat-and-sun crop and "cold-hardy varieties" for the cool south, when in truth this is a humidity-loving, acid-soil understorey shrub that is frost-tender and sold only as unnamed seedlings.

- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives, which hold five miracle fruit articles spanning 1980 to 1997 (the North Queensland introduction, the Cairns nursery cultivation notes, propagation and frost, the history of the taste-modifying protein, and container growing), then cross-checked against the California Rare Fruit Growers, Daleys Fruit Trees, the WA Rare Fruit Club and DPIRD Western Australia.

- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: acid soil at roughly pH 4.5 to 5.8 (think blueberry) or the plant slowly dies of chlorosis in limey ground; one seedling fruits on its own, so you do not need a pair; water drives fruit size, with a dry plant giving tiny, pulpless berries; the main crop comes in the cooler months and flower to ripe berry takes only three to four weeks; and the one pest worth naming is scale, which should be treated with something other than white oil because white oil strips this plant's leaves.

- The matcher was quietly missing stock: "Miracle Berry" is the most common retail name (four of the seven nurseries that list it use it), but the site only recognised "Miracle Fruit", so every "Miracle Berry" listing was invisible. Adding the synonym fixes that, so the species page and the dashboard search now find real in-stock plants that were being skipped.
