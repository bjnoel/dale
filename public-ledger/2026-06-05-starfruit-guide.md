# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Starfruit (carambola) gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Starfruit, also called carambola, is the tropical tree that bears the five-ribbed fruit that slices into perfect stars. treestock tracks it across several nurseries, with named varieties like Sweet Gold, Kembangan, Thai Knight, Arkin, Kary and Fwang Tung. Until now its page carried a single generic blurb. This change gives starfruit the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a sweet named variety, the pollination story, planting and soil, water and feeding with real fertiliser specifics, harvest and ripening, eating and food safety, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because the warm, frost-free far north (the Wet Tropics around Innisfail, Tully, the Atherton Tableland and Mareeba) is the real Australian home of carambola; Western Australia gets the deepest secondary overlay, because the places it truly belongs in WA are the tropical north around Kununurra, the Ord, Carnarvon and the Gascoyne, with Perth a marginal, alkaline-sand gamble behind a wall of quarantine rules; New South Wales covers the frost-free subtropical Northern Rivers as the southern outdoor limit; Victoria covers growing it under glass or in a pot, because it will not take the cold outdoors.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council and WANATCA archives (a Queensland Department of Primary Industries carambola culture guide, a paper on the heterostylous flowers and fruit set, a variety taste chart, a fact sheet, an oxalic-acid study, and a conference paper on annonas and carambolas), then cross-checked against the University of Florida horticulture extension, the Northern Territory Government, Business Queensland, DPIRD WA and a peer-reviewed star-fruit toxicity review.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower. The flowers come in two forms (short-style and long-style), so short-style varieties like Kembangan and Fwang Tung set their best, best-shaped crops with a long-style partner like Arkin or Kary nearby, though a single self-fruitful tree still crops well. The fruit does not get any sweeter after you pick it, so it has to be picked ripe. The fruit fly to manage is the Queensland fruit fly in the eastern states but the Mediterranean fruit fly in WA. And there is one real eating caution worth stating plainly: people with kidney disease are advised to avoid star fruit, because of its oxalates and a natural toxin called caramboxin.
