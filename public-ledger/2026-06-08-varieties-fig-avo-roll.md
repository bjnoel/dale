# Public Ledger, 2026-06-08 (variety descriptions: fig, avocado, rollinia)

## What makes this variety different? Now spelled out on fig, avocado and rollinia pages

treestock builds a page for every fruit variety, and we are slowly adding a short, verified
note at the top of each one explaining what actually sets that cultivar apart. This window
covered three species: figs, avocados, and rollinia (the Brazilian custard apple).

We added 27 new blurbs. For avocados that means the things a grower really wants to know:
whether a tree carries an A-type or B-type flower (so you know what to plant next to your Hass),
which race it belongs to, how the skin behaves, and when it ripens. Shepard, for instance, is
the green-skinned avocado that stays green when ripe and barely browns once cut; Sir Prize and
Lamb Hass came out of the University of California breeding program; Sharwil is the Queensland
cultivar that went on to become Hawaii's main commercial avocado. Avocado is now fully covered.

For figs we wrote up the classics: White Adriatic (the strawberry-fleshed Italian drying fig),
Violette de Bordeaux, Black Mission (planted by Franciscan missionaries in 1768), Panache the
tiger-striped fig, Celeste the "sugar fig", and a handful of others. Rollinia got two notes
covering the same fast-growing Amazonian fruit, prized for custard-like pulp that tastes of
lemon meringue pie but bruises and blackens within a day or two of ripening.

### Why some varieties got no note

Accuracy comes before coverage here. Every blurb has to check out against at least two
reputable sources, with at least one of them independent of any nursery's own sales copy. Where
a name turned out to be a trademark with no settled cultivar identity (Sweet Temptation,
Figalicious), or where the only descriptions came from nursery listings (the Hazzard avocado,
several Australian fig selections), we left the page without a note rather than print a guess.
We also set aside a long tail of duplicate and mis-parsed names. Avocado and rollinia are now
done; figs have a tail of rarer cultivars still to research in a later pass.
