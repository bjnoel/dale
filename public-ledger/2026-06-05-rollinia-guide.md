# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Rollinia (biriba) gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Rollinia, also called biriba or Brazilian custard apple, is the soft, cream-yellow Amazonian fruit that tastes like lemon meringue pie. It is a close cousin of the custard apple, and treestock tracks it at Daleys, Ladybird and Ross Creek Tropicals, sold mostly as seedlings with a few named grafted selections like Picone, Limberlost and Sputnik. Until now its page carried a single generic blurb. This change gives rollinia the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a much richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a seedling or named selection, the pollination story, planting and soil, water and feeding and humidity, harvest and eating, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because the warm, humid, frost-free far north is the one part of Australia where rollinia is really at home, on both the wet tropical coast (Cairns, Innisfail, Tully, Cardwell) and the elevated Atherton Tablelands (Julatten, Mareeba), where some of the country's first trees fruited. New South Wales covers the frost-free subtropical Northern Rivers (Murwillumbah, the Tweed, Lismore, Alstonville) as the southern limit. Western Australia is honest that the tree is a collector's challenge here, because it wants tropical heat and humidity together and WA offers one without the other (a dry north, a Mediterranean Perth). Victoria covers growing it under glass, because frost kills it outdoors.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives (the North Queensland growers' accounts from Julatten and the Johnstone River, including the original 1980s notes), and the WANATCA ACOTANC papers on annonas and on underexploited Philippine fruits, then cross-checked against the University of Florida horticulture extension, the University of Hawaii, Julia Morton's classic fruit reference, Custard Apples Australia, Business Queensland, DPIRD WA and Daleys.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower. One tree will fruit on its own, but the flowers open female first and male later, so natural fruit set can be poor and a little hand pollination lifts the crop, just as it does on cherimoya and atemoya. The tree is not frost tolerant: even a light frost, around 3 degrees on the grass, can kill a young tree, so it needs a genuinely frost-free spot. And the ripe fruit is so perishable, softening almost to a liquid within a day or two and bruising at a touch, that it cannot be sold at market, which is exactly why it is a backyard tree and why planting your own is the only dependable way to taste it.
