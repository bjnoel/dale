# Public Ledger, 2026-06-04

## Wax jambu gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Wax jambu (the crisp, bell-shaped, faintly watermelon-like tropical fruit, also sold as wax apple or Java apple) is a collector favourite, and treestock tracks it across several nurseries. Until now its page shared a single generic blurb. This change gives wax jambu the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (telling wax jambu apart from its lookalikes, choosing a variety, pollination, planting and soil, water and feeding, harvest and ripening, eating and storing, buying tips, and protecting the thin-skinned fruit) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Queensland leads, because the humid far-north wet tropics are the tree's real Australian home; Western Australia gets the standout overlay, since the only place it truly belongs in WA is the tropical Kimberley around Kununurra, with Perth a sheltered-microclimate gamble and a wall of quarantine rules in the way; New South Wales covers the frost-free Northern Rivers as the southern limit; Victoria covers growing it under glass or in a pot, because it will not take the cold outdoors.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own WANATCA and Rare Fruit Council archives (including a 1989 yearbook paper on edible Syzygium fruits and a Southeast Asian fruits paper), then cross-checked against Julia Morton, the World Agroforestry and Taiwan wax-apple references, the Northern Territory Government, Business Queensland, DPIRD WA and Fruit Fly Identification Australia.
- Gets the naming right, which trips up a lot of buyers: true wax jambu is Syzygium samarangense, and it is kept clearly distinct from its close cousins the water apple, the rose apple and the Malay apple, which are all sold under overlapping names.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: one tree fruits on its own, the fruit must ripen on the tree (it will not sweeten after picking), the fruit fly to manage is the Queensland fruit fly in the east but the Mediterranean fruit fly in WA, WA is still essentially free of myrtle rust, and the tree is killed by frost.
