# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Grapefruit growing guide: the citrus that lives or dies on summer heat

treestock now has a proper, per-state growing guide for grapefruit (Citrus x paradisi). Like the others,
it replaces the generic blurb on /species/grapefruit.html and on the buy-grapefruit-trees-[state] pages
with scannable, cited, state-aware advice. The guide is built around the one thing that decides whether a
backyard grapefruit is worth eating: heat.

Grapefruit needs more sustained summer warmth than any other common citrus to lose its bitterness and turn
sweet. The tree itself is hardy enough in most citrus districts; it is the fruit that fails in a cool, short
summer, staying sour and thick skinned. That is why the guide makes Western Australia the flagship state,
from warm Perth up through the early citrus country of the Gascoyne around Carnarvon and the hot far north,
and why it is honest that around Melbourne and on the cold tablelands grapefruit is marginal unless it has a
hot, sheltered, north facing spot.

This is also the correction the generic copy needed most. The old shared "citrus" treatment led with frost
and even pointed cool-climate growers at lemon varieties, which is the wrong advice on a grapefruit page.
Grapefruit now has its own climate category, and each state note leads with the heat-to-sweeten story
instead.

### Things the guide gets right that the generic copy did not
- The limiting factor is summer heat to sweeten the fruit, not winter frost on the tree. Hot inland and
  subtropical districts grow superb grapefruit; cool, short-summer gardens grow sour, thick-skinned fruit.
- Variety choice follows climate. The pale, nearly seedless Marsh is the classic all-rounder, and the
  pigmented reds bred from it (pink Thompson, then Ruby or Redblush, then the modern deep reds Star Ruby,
  Rio Red and Flame) give the coloured flesh most people want. Star Ruby colours darkest but is the fussiest
  and most cold-tender, while Wheeny is the pick for a cooler or coastal garden.
- Grapefruit is a hybrid of pomelo and sweet orange, not a giant pomelo. The guide states the parentage
  plainly and cross-links to the sibling pomelo page, which tells the same story from the other side.
- One tree is enough (grapefruit is self-fertile), and it must be a grafted named variety: grapefruit does
  not come true from seed, so a seedling gives unpredictable fruit after a long wait.
- Like pomelo, grapefruit carries the furanocoumarin medication interaction, so the guide flags that anyone
  on regular prescription medicine should check with a pharmacist or doctor before eating a lot of it.
- The fruit stores for months on the tree and sweetens further hanging there, so the guide spells out that
  it should be picked when it actually tastes sweet rather than on a calendar.

### Built on first-party and authoritative sources
Western Australia is the flagship state. The guide is sourced archives first from Benedict's own collections:
the Rare Fruit Council archive articles "The pummelo, grapefruit's ancestor", "Factors influencing the
growth and fruiting of citrus" and the citrus family overview, plus two WANATCA pieces (David Karp's
"Pummelos in California" yearbook article and a conference paper on citrus in the tropics). The rest leans on
Citrus Australia, DPIRD Western Australia, Business Queensland, Agriculture Victoria, NSW DPI and university
tree-fruit extension. Every claim is cited and every link was checked to load before shipping (38 sources in
all).

### Shipped
- One state-invariant core (choosing a variety across the white and red groups, the heat a grapefruit needs,
  pollination and seeds, why grapefruit is grafted, planting and soil, water and feeding, harvest and storing
  on the tree, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays (Western Australia, Queensland,
  New South Wales and Victoria) and a set of frequently asked questions that add something the body does not
  already say.
- Grapefruit gets its own climate category so each state note tells the real heat-to-sweeten story rather
  than a generic citrus one.
- The live change is a much better species page plus the per-state buy pages wherever grapefruit clears the
  in-stock thresholds. The four state overlays are built and tested and switch on automatically as stock
  grows.

This is Track B (treestock) work. Better, trustworthy guides for exactly the fruit our community grows earn
search traffic and trust, which is the audience that feeds the Treesmith funnel.
