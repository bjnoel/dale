# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Feijoa gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Feijoa (Acca sellowiana, the "pineapple guava") is a collector favourite that treestock tracks across several nurseries, with named varieties like Apollo, Unique, Duffy, Mammoth, Nazemetz and Triumph in stock. Until now its page shared a single generic blurb. This change gives feijoa the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, plus a richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a variety, pollination by birds and which cultivars are self-fertile, planting and sheltering the brittle wood, water and feeding, harvest and ripening, eating and using the fruit and flowers, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Western Australia leads, because it is the only state generating a live buy page today, it is Benedict's home turf, and feijoa genuinely thrives across the cooler south-west; Queensland covers why feijoa belongs in the cool Granite Belt and Darling Downs rather than the warm coast; New South Wales covers the cool tablelands and the more marginal humid north coast; Victoria covers the cool-climate sweet spot where feijoa fruits best of all.
- Feijoa is given its own climate category rather than being lumped in with subtropical fruit. That generic label was exactly wrong for it: feijoa is one of the most cold-hardy of the exotic fruits (down to about minus 10 degrees), it actually needs a cool winter to fruit well, and it tastes best in cooler districts, so it crops best in the south and only poorly in the warm, humid tropics. Each state note now tells that real story.
- Sourced archives-first from Benedict's own WANATCA archives, including Martin Crawford's yearbook article on the feijoa and Neville Passmore's ACOTANC paper ("Feijoa is a superb fruit for the Perth area. It is one of the most cold-tolerant of all the exotic fruits."), plus the Rare Fruit Council of Australia feijoa archive. Gaps were filled and cross-checked against Daleys, University of Florida IFAS Extension, the New Zealand Tree Crops Association, Tui, Business Queensland, DPIRD WA and Fruit Fly Identification Australia.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: a few cultivars (Unique, Apollo) crop on their own but every feijoa bears more heavily with a second variety nearby, birds do the pollinating while nibbling the sweet edible petals, fruit reaches full flavour only as it drops (so it is gathered fresh off the ground, not picked hard and green), and the pest to manage is the Queensland fruit fly in the east and the Mediterranean fruit fly in WA.
