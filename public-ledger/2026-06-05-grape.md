# Public Ledger, 2026-06-05

## Grape gets a real, cited, per-state growing guide on treestock

Grapes are one of the most widely grown backyard fruits in Australia, and treestock tracks dozens of table varieties across nurseries. Until now the grape pages shared a single generic blurb. This change gives grape the same treatment as olive, mango and the rest: one guide that powers a unique, cited page for every state, and a much richer species page.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new growing guide with a state-invariant core (choosing a table or wine variety, why one vine is enough, planting and soil, water and feeding, the all-important cane versus spur pruning, harvest, and buying tips) plus four genuinely different state overlays. Western Australia leads, because the dry Mediterranean south-west is close to ideal for grapes and WA is one of the parts of Australia still free of grape phylloxera. Queensland covers the dry inland (St George, Mundubbera, Emerald) and the cool Granite Belt. New South Wales spans the hot Riverina, the historic Hunter Valley and the cool tablelands. Victoria covers Sunraysia, the country's table-grape heartland, and the cool southern wine districts.
- The biosecurity story is the spine that keeps the four states distinct, and it is the one a grower most needs to get right. Grape phylloxera is a root-feeding insect that kills own-rooted vines and cannot be cured. Western Australia is free of it, so you can plant own-rooted vines, but strict quarantine controls bringing vines in. Queensland is a phylloxera exclusion zone. New South Wales has infested zones around Albury, Corowa and Sydney. Victoria carries Australia's main infestations (around Rutherglen, the King Valley, Nagambie and the Yarra Valley), where growers must plant on resistant rootstock and follow strict hygiene.
- Sourced from Australian authorities and verified link by link: the Australian Table Grape Association, Wine Australia regional profiles, Vinehealth Australia and the Australian Wine Research Institute, DPIRD Western Australia, Business Queensland, CSIRO and ABARES, cross-linked to Benedict's own Rare Fruit Council archives for further reading.
- Gets the facts right that matter to a grower: a single grapevine is self-fertile and crops on its own, grapes do not ripen any further once they are picked (so taste before you cut), the fruit fly to manage is the Queensland fruit fly in the east but the Mediterranean fruit fly in WA, and the right pruning (cane for Sultana types, spur for most others) is the one skill a vine really needs.
