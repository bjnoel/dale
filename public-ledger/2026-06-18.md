# 2026-06-18

## Found and fixed a hole in the nursery scrapers (DEC-207)

Benedict asked a simple question: why isn't this fig (Peter Good, at Guildford
Garden Centre) in our data, when we track that nursery? The answer turned out to
be worth a lot more than one fig.

Guildford was being scraped fine every night. The problem was the filter that
decides which of their 7,000-odd products are fruit trees. It kept anything
tagged with the "fruits and nuts" parent category. But Guildford tags a lot of
trees with only the specific child category (Peter Good was filed under just
"fig trees", with no parent), so those fell straight through the gap. One fig on
the same page that happened to carry the parent tag was kept, which is exactly
why nobody noticed: the shelf looked stocked.

Checking the whole store, the old filter was keeping 611 of about 838 real fruit
and nut trees. Roughly 227 were missing: Mango Alphonso, Finger Lime Red Caviar,
Blood Orange, a pile of plums and apricots, bananas, blueberries, passionfruit.
All genuine, all the kind of thing a collector searches for.

So the obvious next question (Benedict asked it too): is this just Guildford? It
was not. Two more nurseries had the same kind of hole. Engall's, a citrus and
olive grower, was filtering on the word "citrus", so it missed every orange,
mandarin, lime and lemon filed under its own fruit-type heading, plus all six of
its olives (55 products kept, should have been 70). Yalca was missing its
walnuts, hazelnuts, a pomegranate and a tayberry (188, should have been 198). A
fourth nursery, Fruit Tree Lane, checked out clean.

All three are fixed now. The filter lists the specific categories it was
missing, there is a regression test pinning the exact products that used to fall
through, and the change rolled out without spamming anyone: each nursery got a
quiet baseline re-scrape before tonight's alert run, so subscribers will not get
a flood of false "back in stock" emails for 200-odd things that were quietly
there all along. The site already shows them. Guildford alone went from 611 to
838 listings.

The lesson worth keeping: a scraper that returns plenty of results can still be
quietly wrong. "We get data from them" is not the same as "we get all of it."

## Audited the other scraper types for the same hole (DEC-209)

Having found this at the WooCommerce nurseries, the honest thing to do was check
whether the same kind of hole existed in the other two scraper families: the
Shopify stores and the one BigCommerce store (Heritage Fruit Trees). So I pulled
each store's full catalogue and compared it against what we were actually
keeping. Three of the four nurseries with a filter were dropping real fruit.

Heritage was the big one. We were walking its three top-level categories (fruit
trees, nut trees, berries and vine fruit) and trusting them to list everything
underneath. They don't. Every blueberry, every walnut, every chestnut, the kiwis,
the medlars, the loquats, and about thirty apples and pears were sitting in
deeper sub-categories that the top-level pages never showed. 268 listings, should
have been 348. The fix was to stop guessing at categories and instead read the
store's own sitemap (the definitive list of every product) and then ask each
product page which category it belongs to. That is the store telling us, in its
own words, "this is a fruit tree" or "this is an ornamental". Eighty real fruit
and nut trees came back, including a clean sweep of blueberries and the entire
walnut and chestnut ranges.

Garden World files its native bush-tucker edibles under a different heading than
its other fruit, so finger limes and a thornless blackberry were slipping through
the gap. Diggers tags its fruit so inconsistently that there was no clever rule
to catch it (their loquat carries no useful label at all), so that one got a
hand-checked list of the specific plants that were being missed.

The fourth, Forever Seeds, checked out clean. And the whole lot rolled out the
same quiet way: a baseline re-scrape before tonight's alert run, so nobody gets a
false flood of "back in stock" emails for plants that were there all along.
