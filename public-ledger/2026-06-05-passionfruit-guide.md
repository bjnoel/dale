# Public Ledger — 2026-06-05

## Passionfruit growing guide added to treestock, flagship Queensland, with the WA quarantine story told straight

Passionfruit is one of the most-loved backyard vines in Australia, but its treestock pages shared a generic blurb and were about to inherit a "subtropical" climate label that missed everything that actually matters for this crop. This change gives passionfruit the same cited, per-state growing guide the other species have, led by Queensland (the heartland, which grows most of the national crop), and fixes the climate story state by state.

### Shipped (pending review)
- A new, fully cited passionfruit guide (23 sources) with a shared core (choosing a variety, pollination and self-fertility, planting and support, water and feeding, harvest and eating, woodiness virus and a short vine life, and buying grafted vs seedling) plus a genuinely different overlay for Queensland, New South Wales, Western Australia and Victoria.
- The advice is tied to reality. Varieties are matched to what is actually in stock and split into the two real groups: purple and black types (Misty Gem, Sweetheart, Pandora and the grafted Nellie Kelly, Passiflora edulis) for the rich, sweet-tart flavour on a cooler-tolerant vine, and the larger Panama and golden types (Panama Red, Panama Gold) for size and tropical heat tolerance. Pollination is told straight and corrected against the research: Australian purple hybrids are self-fertile, so a single vine crops, while the golden and Panama types are more self-incompatible and set far better with a second vine. Carpenter bees are named as the most efficient pollinators, with honeybees effective too.
- Passionfruit now has its OWN climate category instead of inheriting "subtropical", so each state's note tells the real story: Queensland is the warm, frost-free heartland; the New South Wales north coast suits it while frost limits the inland; Western Australia has Mediterranean fruit fly (not Queensland fruit fly) and a quarantine hurdle on live vines; and Victoria is marginal and leans on a grafted, cold-tolerant vine.
- Each state gets its own story. Queensland leads as the flagship (subtropical south-east for the purple types, the humid tropical coast to Cooktown and the Daintree for the Panama types, brown spot and Queensland fruit fly as the headline problems). New South Wales is the warm north coast around the Tweed and Coffs Harbour, with the passionvine hopper and its sooty mould. Western Australia is the standout overlay: passionfruit has been grown from Albany to Perth since the 1920s, Mediterranean fruit fly is the local fruit fly, and the strict quarantine that filters which nurseries can ship live vines here is explained, with seed-raised and local stock offered as the way around it. Victoria is the cool-climate gamble, grown on a grafted Nellie Kelly against a hot wall, with the classic warning that the blue passionflower rootstock will take over and give small, hard fruit if its suckers are left to grow.
- The feeding section is specific, not generic: a balanced fertiliser with potassium at least equal to nitrogen (about 200 grams of a 10:3:10 blend per bearing vine each month, plus a little boron), up to 140 litres a week at peak in summer, and a soil pH of 5.5 to 6.5, all from state agriculture department sources.
- Further reading keeps authority in network: the Rare Fruit Council of Australia archives (the passionfruit cultivation and Genus Passiflora articles) and a WANATCA yearbook article (Beal and Farlow, 1982).
